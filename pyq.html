Q.A1. What is the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics?

Detailed Explanation: The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics The Zeroth Law is the most fundamental concept in thermodynamics because it defines the very meaning of temperature. It was formulated after the First and Second Laws, but it logically precedes them, hence the name "Zeroth."

1. Statement of the Law:

The law states: "If two thermodynamic systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other."

2. Step-wise Understanding:

Imagine three bodies: A, B, and C.
  • Step 1: System A is brought into contact with System C. If no net heat flows between them, they are in thermal equilibrium. We write this as $A \leftrightarrow C$.
  • Step 2: System B is brought into contact with System C. If no net heat flows between them, they are also in thermal equilibrium. We write this as $B \leftrightarrow C$.
  • Conclusion (Zeroth Law): The law dictates that if you now bring System A and System B into contact, they must also be in thermal equilibrium ($A \leftrightarrow B$).

3. Physical Significance (Defining Temperature):

The most crucial significance of this law is that it establishes that there exists a property, which we call temperature ($T$), that determines whether a system is in thermal equilibrium with another. The law implies that all systems in thermal equilibrium share the same value for this property (temperature). This is the underlying principle that allows us to use a thermometer (the third system, C) to accurately measure and compare the temperatures of any two other bodies (A and B).

Q.A2. What do you understand by Thermodynamic Equilibrium?

Detailed Explanation: Thermodynamic Equilibrium (The State of Perfect Balance) Thermodynamic equilibrium describes a state of a system where all macroscopic properties are perfectly stable and do not change over time. It is a state of perfect balance where no potential difference exists to drive any process. To be in complete thermodynamic equilibrium, a system must simultaneously satisfy three types of equilibrium:

1. Thermal Equilibrium (Balance of Temperature):

  • Condition: The temperature ($T$) must be uniform throughout the entire system.
  • Implication: There are no temperature gradients, meaning there is no spontaneous tendency for heat transfer to occur from one part of the system to another.

2. Mechanical Equilibrium (Balance of Forces):

  • Condition: The pressure ($P$) must be uniform throughout the system, and there must be no unbalanced forces.
  • Implication: This prevents the system from undergoing spontaneous bulk motion (like a piston moving) or volume change. For example, if the pressure inside a balloon isn't balanced by the external pressure, it's not in mechanical equilibrium.

3. Chemical Equilibrium (Balance of Composition):

  • Condition: The chemical composition of the system must not change over time.
  • Implication: This prevents any chemical reactions or phase transitions (like melting or evaporation) from occurring spontaneously. The number of moles of each chemical component remains constant.
Only when a system is in Thermal, Mechanical, AND Chemical Equilibrium is it considered to be in complete Thermodynamic Equilibrium.

Q.A3. What do you mean by a Heat Engine? What is its efficiency?

Detailed Explanation: Heat Engine and Its Efficiency A Heat Engine is essentially a device that bridges the gap between thermal energy (heat) and mechanical energy (work).

1. Definition of a Heat Engine:

A heat engine is a device that operates in a thermodynamic cycle (meaning it returns to its initial state) and converts a portion of the heat energy supplied to it from a high-temperature source into useful mechanical work.

2. Essential Components (The Cycle):

A heat engine must interact with three parts:
  • Source (High-Temperature Reservoir, $T_1$): Provides the heat input ($Q_1$).
  • Working Substance: The material (like steam in a turbine or gasoline in an engine) that absorbs the heat, expands, and performs the work.
  • Sink (Low-Temperature Reservoir, $T_2$): Receives the rejected heat ($Q_2$) that cannot be converted into work (due to the Second Law).

3. The Efficiency ($\eta$):

The efficiency is a measure of how good the engine is at converting the input heat into the output work. Step 1: Definition in terms of Work and Heat Input: $$\eta = \frac{\text{Work Done by the Engine }(W)}{\text{Heat Absorbed from the Source }(Q_1)}$$ Step 2: Applying the First Law of Thermodynamics: For a complete cycle, the change in internal energy ($\Delta U$) is zero. Thus, the work done ($W$) is the difference between the heat absorbed ($Q_1$) and the heat rejected ($Q_2$): $$W = Q_1 - Q_2$$ Step 3: Final Efficiency Expression (in terms of Heat): Substituting the expression for $W$: $$\eta = \frac{Q_1 - Q_2}{Q_1} = 1 - \frac{Q_2}{Q_1}$$ Note: For a Carnot Engine (the theoretical maximum efficiency), the efficiency can also be written in terms of absolute temperatures ($T$): $$\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$ Since $T_2$ is always greater than absolute zero, $\eta$ is always less than 1 (or 100%), which is a statement of the Second Law.

Q.A4. Show that entropy remains constant in a reversible cycle.

Detailed Explanation: Entropy Change in a Reversible Cycle This proof relies on the fact that Entropy ($S$) is a state function.

1. Definition of Entropy Change:

Entropy change ($\Delta S$) is defined for a reversible process as the ratio of the heat supplied reversibly ($dQ_{rev}$) to the absolute temperature ($T$): $$dS = \frac{dQ_{rev}}{T}$$

2. The Concept of a State Function:

A state function (like $S$, $U$, $H$, $G$) depends only on the initial and final states of the system, and not on the path taken between them.

3. Applying the Concept to a Cycle:

A cyclic process is one where the system starts at an initial state (State 1) and, after a series of changes, returns exactly to the same initial state. Since the final state is identical to the initial state (State 1 = State 2), the change in any state function must be zero.

4. Mathematical Proof (Clausius Theorem):

For a small change in a cycle, the total change in entropy is: $$\Delta S_{\text{total}} = \int_{\text{initial}}^{\text{final}} dS$$ For a complete cyclic process, the integral is written as a closed loop: $$\oint dS$$ Since $S$ is a state function: $$\oint dS = 0$$ Substituting the definition of $dS$ for a reversible cycle: $$\oint \frac{dQ_{rev}}{T} = 0$$ Conclusion: This famous integral, derived from the Clausius inequality for a reversible cycle, mathematically proves that the net change in entropy of a system undergoing a reversible cycle is zero. This means the entropy of the system remains conserved throughout the reversible process.

Q.A5. What is a perfectly black body? How can it be realized in practice?

Detailed Explanation: Perfectly Black Body and Its Practical Realization A perfectly black body is an idealized theoretical concept crucial to understanding thermal radiation.

1. Definition of a Perfectly Black Body:

A black body is defined by two properties:
  • Perfect Absorber (Absorption): It absorbs all electromagnetic radiation (light, heat, radio waves, etc.) incident upon it, regardless of the wavelength, intensity, or angle. Its absorptivity ($a$) is exactly 1.
  • Perfect Emitter (Emission): When heated, it emits the maximum possible amount of thermal radiation at every frequency for a given temperature. It is the best possible emitter, and its emissivity ($\epsilon$) is also 1.
Note: According to Kirchhoff's Law of Radiation, a good absorber is also a good emitter.

2. Practical Realization (Ferry's Black Body):

While no material is a perfectly black body in reality, we can create a near-perfect model using a device called Ferry's Black Body. The Setup: The practical black body is a hollow enclosure (like a metal sphere or cylinder) with a very small opening (aperture). The inner walls are coated with a black material (like lamp black) which is a decent absorber. The Mechanism (Why it works): When radiation enters the small hole:
  • It strikes the inner wall and is partially absorbed (e.g., 90%).
  • The remaining radiation (10%) is reflected to another point on the inner wall.
  • At the second point, 90% of the remaining 10% is absorbed, and so on.
Due to the multiple internal reflections and absorptions, the probability of any radiation escaping back out of the tiny hole is virtually zero. The small aperture itself acts as the perfectly black surface, absorbing all incident energy. When this cavity is heated, the radiation emitted from the hole is considered to be nearly perfect black-body radiation.

Q.A6. What do you mean by microstate and macrostate?

Detailed Explanation: Microstate vs. Macrostate (The Statistical View) These concepts are the heart of Statistical Mechanics, which links the microscopic world of atoms and molecules to the macroscopic world of pressure and temperature.

1. Macrostate (The Big Picture):

  • Definition: A macrostate is a state of a system described by its observable, bulk properties (macroscopic parameters).
  • Examples: Total Energy ($E$), Volume ($V$), Number of Particles ($N$), Pressure ($P$), and Temperature ($T$).
  • Key Feature: A macrostate defines the condition of the system that we can measure with instruments.

2. Microstate (The Detailed Picture):

  • Definition: A microstate is a complete and precise description of the system at the atomic or molecular level.
  • Classical View: This means specifying the exact position and momentum of every single particle in the system.
  • Quantum View: This means specifying the exact quantum state (e.g., energy level) occupied by every particle in the system.
  • Key Feature: Microstates are the tiny, specific arrangements that make up a macrostate.

3. The Relationship (Thermodynamic Probability):

The crucial link is that many different microstates can correspond to the same macrostate. The number of microstates corresponding to a given macrostate is called the Thermodynamic Probability or Weight ($\Omega$). Example: For a gas in a box with fixed $V$ and $E$, every possible arrangement of the particles' positions and velocities that adds up to the total energy $E$ is a different microstate, but they all result in the same observed macrostate (same $P, T$). The macrostate with the highest $\Omega$ is the one the system is most likely to be in at equilibrium, leading to the Boltzmann Entropy Formula: $$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$

Q.A7. State Stefan–Boltzmann Law of radiation.

Detailed Explanation: The Stefan–Boltzmann Law of Radiation This law quantifies the total energy radiated by a black body.

1. Statement of the Law:

The Stefan–Boltzmann Law states that the total radiant heat energy emitted per unit surface area per unit time (called the total emissive power, $E$) of a perfectly black body is directly proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature ($T$).

2. Mathematical Expression:

$$E = \sigma T^4$$ Where:
  • $E$: Total energy emitted per square meter per second ($\text{W}/\text{m}^2$).
  • $T$: The absolute temperature of the black body (in Kelvin, K).
  • $\sigma$: The Stefan–Boltzmann Constant. Its value is $\sigma \approx 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W} \cdot \text{m}^{-2} \cdot \text{K}^{-4}$.

3. Significance:

The dependence on $T^4$ is extremely steep. This means a small increase in temperature leads to a massive increase in the emitted radiation power. Example: Doubling the absolute temperature ($2T$) increases the emitted energy by a factor of $2^4 = 16$. This is why the sun (very high $T$) radiates so much more energy than the Earth (lower $T$).

4. For Non-Black Bodies:

For a real object (a gray body) with emissivity $\epsilon$ (where $0 < \epsilon < 1$): $$E_{\text{real}} = \epsilon \sigma T^4$$

Q.A8. What is the Rayleigh–Jeans Law?

Detailed Explanation: The Rayleigh–Jeans Law (A Classical Failure) The Rayleigh–Jeans Law was a classical physics formula (before quantum mechanics) that attempted to describe the intensity of radiation emitted by a black body at a given temperature.

1. Classical Basis:

The law was derived using two classical principles:
  • The laws of classical electromagnetism to find the number of standing wave modes (oscillators) inside a black-body cavity.
  • The Equipartition Theorem of classical thermodynamics, which states that every mode should have an average energy of $k_B T$.

2. Mathematical Expression (in terms of Wavelength $\lambda$):

The spectral energy density $u(\lambda)$ (energy per unit volume per unit wavelength) is given by: $$u(\lambda, T) = \frac{8\pi k_B T}{\lambda^4}$$

3. The Problem: The Ultraviolet Catastrophe:

The Rayleigh–Jeans Law agreed well with experimental data for long wavelengths (low frequencies). However, it failed dramatically for short wavelengths (high frequencies, i.e., the ultraviolet region and beyond). According to the formula: $$\text{As } \lambda \to 0, \quad u(\lambda, T) \to \infty$$ This predicted that a black body should emit an infinite amount of energy at short wavelengths, which is clearly impossible and contradicts observation. This theoretical disaster was famously named the Ultraviolet Catastrophe.

4. Resolution:

The failure of the Rayleigh–Jeans law forced physicists to abandon the classical assumption of continuous energy and paved the way for Planck's Quantum Hypothesis (Q.B6), where energy is quantized ($E = h\nu$).

Q.B1. Formulate the First Law of Thermodynamics and explain its physical significance.

Detailed Explanation: The First Law of Thermodynamics and its Significance The First Law is simply the principle of Conservation of Energy applied to thermodynamic systems. It describes how energy is transformed between heat, work, and internal energy.

1. Formulation of the First Law (Mathematical Statement)

The law states that the change in the internal energy ($\Delta U$) of a closed thermodynamic system is equal to the heat energy ($Q$) supplied to the system minus the work ($W$) done by the system on its surroundings. $$\Delta U = Q - W$$ Alternative convention (Work done ON the system): If $W$ represents work done on the system (e.g., compressing a gas), the formula is $\Delta U = Q + W$. We will use the former convention ($\Delta U = Q - W$). For a small change in state: $$dU = dQ - dW$$ Where:
  • $dU$ (or $\Delta U$): The change in Internal Energy (a state function).
  • $dQ$ (or $Q$): The heat added to the system (a path function).
  • $dW$ (or $W$): The work done by the system (a path function, often $P dV$).

2. Physical Significance and Implications

A. The Law of Conservation of Energy:

The most profound significance is that the First Law mathematically formalizes the idea that energy is conserved. Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be converted from one form (heat $Q$) into another (work $W$) or stored (internal energy $U$).

B. Introduction of Internal Energy ($U$):

The First Law defines Internal Energy ($U$) as a fundamental property of the system. * $U$ is a State Function: The change in internal energy ($\Delta U$) depends only on the initial and final states of the system ($U_{\text{final}} - U_{\text{initial}}$), regardless of the path taken (how much heat $Q$ was added or work $W$ was done). This means $dU$ is an exact differential. * Energy and Temperature: For an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature ($U \propto T$). For real systems, it also slightly depends on volume and pressure.

C. Distinction between Heat and Work:

The law makes it clear that $Q$ and $W$ are not state functions; they are path functions. The amount of heat exchanged and work done depends entirely on the specific process (path) the system takes between the initial and final states.

D. Exclusion of Perpetual Motion Machines (Type I):

A device that could produce work ($W > 0$) without any external energy input ($Q=0$) would require a spontaneous creation of internal energy ($\Delta U = -W$), violating the First Law. The law makes the Perpetual Motion Machine of the First Kind impossible.

Q.B2. State and prove Carnot’s Theorem.

Detailed Explanation: Carnot's Theorem (The Limit of Efficiency) Carnot's Theorem is a cornerstone of the Second Law of Thermodynamics and sets the ultimate theoretical limit on the efficiency of any heat engine.

1. Statement of Carnot's Theorem

The theorem has two parts, applying to engines operating between a high-temperature source ($T_1$) and a low-temperature sink ($T_2$):

Part I (Maximum Efficiency):

> "No heat engine operating between two given constant temperature reservoirs can have an efficiency greater than a reversible engine (like a Carnot engine) operating between the same two reservoirs." $$\eta_{\text{irreversible}} \le \eta_{\text{reversible}}$$

Part II (Universal Efficiency):

> "The efficiency of all reversible engines operating between the same two temperature reservoirs is the same, regardless of the nature of the working substance." $$\eta_{\text{reversible}} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$

2. Proof by Contradiction

The proof of Part I is typically done by assuming the theorem is false and showing that this leads to a contradiction of the Second Law of Thermodynamics (specifically, the Clausius Statement).

Step 1: The Setup

Assume we have two engines operating between $T_1$ and $T_2$: * $R$: A Reversible Carnot engine (efficiency $\eta_R$). * $I$: A hypothetical Irreversible engine (efficiency $\eta_I$). Let's assume the contradiction is true: $\mathbf{\eta_I > \eta_R}$.

Step 2: Coupling the Engines

1. Engine $I$ is operated forward (as an engine), taking $Q_{1I}$ from $T_1$ and giving out work $W_I$. 2. Engine $R$ is operated in reverse (as a refrigerator), consuming work $W_R$ to lift heat $Q_{2R}$ from $T_2$ to $T_1$. We adjust the engines so that the work done is equal and cancels out: $W_I = W_R = W$.

Step 3: Calculating Heat Flows (The Contradiction)

Since we assumed $\eta_I > \eta_R$ and $W_I = W_R$: $$\frac{W_I}{Q_{1I}} > \frac{W_R}{Q_{1R}} \implies \frac{W}{Q_{1I}} > \frac{W}{Q_{1R}}$$ This means the heat absorbed by $I$ must be less than the heat rejected by $R$: $$Q_{1I} < Q_{1R}$$ Now, calculate the net heat flows for the combined system: * Net Work: $W_{\text{net}} = W_I - W_R = 0$ * Net Heat at $T_1$ (Hot Reservoir): $Q_{\text{net}, 1} = Q_{1R} - Q_{1I}$. Since $Q_{1R} > Q_{1I}$, the net flow is into $T_1$ (heat rejected to the hot reservoir). * Net Heat at $T_2$ (Cold Reservoir): By the First Law, since $W_{\text{net}}=0$, the net heat absorbed from $T_2$ must equal the net heat delivered to $T_1$. Therefore, the system must absorb $Q_{\text{net}, 1}$ from $T_2$.

Step 4: Conclusion

The coupled device absorbs a net amount of heat from the cold reservoir $T_2$ and rejects the entire amount to the hot reservoir $T_1$, with no external work input. This is a direct violation of the Clausius Statement of the Second Law (Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body). Since our conclusion violates a proven law, our initial assumption ($\eta_I > \eta_R$) must be false. Therefore, $\mathbf{\eta_I \le \eta_R}$.

Q.B3. What are Thermodynamic Potentials? Define them and derive Maxwell’s Thermodynamic Relations.

Detailed Explanation: Thermodynamic Potentials and Maxwell’s Relations Thermodynamic Potentials are fundamental state functions that measure the energy available in a thermodynamic system under specific experimental conditions (constraints). They simplify complex thermodynamic processes.

1. Defining the Four Potentials

All potentials are derived from the First Law ($dU = dQ - dW$) combined with the Second Law ($dQ = TdS$ for reversible), giving the fundamental equation: $$dU = TdS - PdV$$ | Potential ($Z$) | Definition | Differential ($dZ$) | Natural Variables | Constant Condition for Equilibrium | | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | | Internal Energy ($U$) | $U$ | $dU = TdS - PdV$ | $S, V$ (Entropy, Volume) | $S, V$ | | Enthalpy ($H$) | $H = U + PV$ | $dH = TdS + VdP$ | $S, P$ (Entropy, Pressure) | $S, P$ | | Helmholtz Free Energy ($F$) | $F = U - TS$ | $dF = -SdT - PdV$ | $T, V$ (Temp, Volume) | $T, V$ | | Gibbs Free Energy ($G$) | $G = H - TS$ | $dG = -SdT + VdP$ | $T, P$ (Temp, Pressure) | $T, P$ | The natural variables are the ones that must be held constant for that potential to indicate the equilibrium state (i.e., its value becomes minimum at equilibrium).

2. Derivation of Maxwell’s Relations

Since $U, H, F, G$ are all state functions, their differentials ($dU, dH, dF, dG$) must be exact differentials. For any exact differential $dZ = Mdx + Ndy$, we must have: $$\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right)_x = \left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial x}\right)_y$$ Applying this cross-differentiation property to the four potentials gives the four Maxwell's Relations: | Potential | Exact Differential ($dZ = Mdx + Ndy$) | Maxwell Relation: $\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right)_x = \left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial x}\right)_y$ | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | $U$ | $dU = \mathbf{T}dS + (\mathbf{-P})dV$ | $\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V}$ (M.R. I) | | $H$ | $dH = \mathbf{T}dS + (\mathbf{V})dP$ | $\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\right)_P}$ (M.R. II) | | $F$ | $dF = \mathbf{-S}dT + (\mathbf{-P})dV$ | $\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V}$ (M.R. III) | | $G$ | $dG = \mathbf{-S}dT + (\mathbf{V})dP$ | $\mathbf{-\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P}$ (M.R. IV) | Significance of Maxwell's Relations: These equations are incredibly useful because they allow us to relate partial derivatives involving unmeasurable quantities (like $S$) to partial derivatives involving easily measurable quantities ($P, V, T$). For example, M.R. III allows us to calculate the change in entropy with respect to volume ($\partial S/\partial V$) simply by measuring the change in pressure with respect to temperature ($\partial P/\partial T$) at constant volume.

Q.B4. Write short notes on the following: a) Clausius–Clapeyron Equation b) Triple Point

Detailed Explanation: Short Notes on Key Phase Concepts

a) Clausius–Clapeyron Equation (Phase Coexistence)

1. Definition and Purpose:

The Clausius–Clapeyron equation is a differential equation that describes the slope of the coexistence curve ($\mathbf{dP/dT}$) between two phases (e.g., solid-liquid, liquid-vapor) of a pure substance on a pressure-temperature ($P-T$) diagram. It shows how the melting point or boiling point changes with pressure.

2. Derivation (Basis):

It is derived from the condition that for two phases (1 and 2) to coexist in equilibrium, their Gibbs Free Energies must be equal ($G_1 = G_2$). A small change along the coexistence curve also requires $dG_1 = dG_2$. Using $dG = -SdT + VdP$: $$dG_1 = -S_1 dT + V_1 dP$$ $$dG_2 = -S_2 dT + V_2 dP$$ Setting $dG_1 = dG_2$: $$-S_1 dT + V_1 dP = -S_2 dT + V_2 dP$$ $$(V_2 - V_1) dP = (S_2 - S_1) dT$$

3. Final Equation:

Since the phase transition occurs at constant temperature $T$, the change in entropy $\Delta S = S_2 - S_1$ is related to the latent heat $L$ (or $\Delta L$) by $\Delta S = L/T$. $$\mathbf{\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T(V_2 - V_1)}}$$ Significance: It allows us to calculate the latent heat ($L$) of a phase transition by measuring only $P, T$, and the volume change ($\Delta V$).

b) Triple Point (Unique Equilibrium)

1. Definition:

The triple point is the unique combination of thermodynamic pressure ($P$) and temperature ($T$) at which the three phases of a substance—solid, liquid, and gas—coexist in stable thermodynamic equilibrium.

2. Characteristics:

* Fixed Point: For any pure substance, the triple point is a unique, unchangeable, and experimentally reproducible point on its $P-T$ diagram. * Water Example: For water, the triple point is $T = 273.16 \text{ K}$ ($0.01^\circ \text{C}$) and $P = 611.73 \text{ Pascals}$. * Gibbs Phase Rule: The rule $F = C - P + 2$ (where $F$ is degrees of freedom, $C$ is components, $P$ is phases) confirms its uniqueness. For the triple point of water ($C=1, P=3$): $$F = 1 - 3 + 2 = 0$$ Zero degrees of freedom means that both pressure and temperature are fixed and cannot be varied independently while maintaining all three phases. Significance: Because the triple point of water is so precisely fixed, it is used as the defining fundamental fixed point for the Kelvin temperature scale.

Q.B5. Derive the expression for Wien’s Displacement Law in terms of thermodynamics.

Detailed Explanation: Derivation of Wien's Displacement Law Wien's Displacement Law describes how the spectrum of black-body radiation shifts with temperature. The Law: The wavelength ($\lambda_{\text{max}}$) at which the energy radiated by a black body is maximum is inversely proportional to its absolute temperature ($T$). $$\mathbf{\lambda_{\text{max}} T = \text{constant} \quad (\text{Wien's constant } b)}$$

1. Thermodynamic Basis (Radiation Pressure)

The derivation treats the black-body radiation inside a cavity as a thermodynamic system (a gas of photons). Key relations used: * Energy Density ($u$): Energy per unit volume, $u = U/V$. * Radiation Pressure ($P$): The pressure exerted by the photons is related to the energy density by $P = \frac{1}{3} u$.

2. General Form of the Black-Body Energy Density

Using the First and Second Laws, it can be rigorously shown (through the use of Maxwell's Relations and scaling arguments) that the energy density function $u(\lambda, T)$ must satisfy a general functional form: $$\mathbf{u(\lambda, T) = \frac{1}{\lambda^5} f(\lambda T)}$$ Where $f(\lambda T)$ is some universal function that depends only on the product of $\lambda$ and $T$. This expression is known as Wien's Distribution Law (in its general form).

3. Finding the Maximum Emission Wavelength ($\lambda_{\text{max}}$)

To find the wavelength where the radiation energy density is maximum, we must differentiate $u(\lambda, T)$ with respect to $\lambda$ and set the derivative to zero: $$\frac{\partial u(\lambda, T)}{\partial \lambda} = 0$$ Substituting the general form: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial \lambda} \left[ \frac{1}{\lambda^5} f(\lambda T) \right] = 0$$ Applying the product rule ($\frac{d}{dx}(uv) = u'v + uv'$), where $u=1/\lambda^5$ and $v=f(\lambda T)$: $$\frac{-5}{\lambda^6} f(\lambda T) + \frac{1}{\lambda^5} f'(\lambda T) \cdot T = 0$$ Multiplying by $\lambda^6$ and rearranging: $$T \lambda f'(\lambda T) = 5 f(\lambda T)$$ Key Insight: Let $x = \lambda T$. The entire equation can be written solely in terms of $x$: $$x f'(x) = 5 f(x)$$ Since the function $f$ is universal, the solution for $x$ that satisfies this differential equation is a unique constant value, let's call it $b$. $$x_{\text{max}} = \lambda_{\text{max}} T = b$$ Conclusion: This rigorously proves that the wavelength of maximum emission is inversely proportional to the absolute temperature, $\mathbf{\lambda_{\text{max}} \propto \frac{1}{T}}$.

Q.B6. Explain Planck’s Quantum Theory of Radiation and derive the expression for the average energy of a Planck oscillator.

Detailed Explanation: Planck's Theory and Average Oscillator Energy Max Planck's theory, introduced in 1900, was the revolutionary concept that solved the Ultraviolet Catastrophe and marked the birth of Quantum Mechanics.

1. Planck’s Quantum Theory of Radiation (Postulates)

Planck made two radical assumptions about the atomic oscillators in the walls of the black-body cavity:

Postulate 1: Quantized Energy

> An atomic oscillator can only possess discrete amounts of energy, which are integer multiples of a fundamental unit of energy ($\mathbf{h\nu}$). $$\mathbf{E_n = n h\nu} \quad (n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \dots)$$ * $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$). * $\nu$ is the frequency of the radiation. * This means the energy of the oscillator is *not* continuous, but comes in packets (quanta).

Postulate 2: Quantized Emission/Absorption

> An oscillator can only absorb or emit energy when it jumps from one allowed energy level to another. The energy exchanged during this jump is exactly one quantum: $\mathbf{\Delta E = h\nu}$.

2. Derivation of the Average Energy ($\langle E \rangle$)

The goal is to calculate the average energy of a single oscillator (with frequency $\nu$) at a temperature $T$, using the statistical method.

Step 1: Using the Boltzmann Distribution

The number of oscillators $N_n$ in the energy state $E_n$ is proportional to the Boltzmann factor $e^{-E_n/k_B T}$: $$N_n \propto e^{-n h\nu / k_B T}$$

Step 2: Formula for Average Energy

The average energy $\langle E \rangle$ is the total energy of all oscillators divided by the total number of oscillators: $$\langle E \rangle = \frac{\sum_{\text{all states}} (\text{Energy of state}) \times (\text{Number of oscillators in state})}{\sum_{\text{all states}} (\text{Number of oscillators in state})}$$ $$\langle E \rangle = \frac{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n h\nu \cdot e^{-n h\nu / k_B T}}{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-n h\nu / k_B T}}$$

Step 3: Simplification using Substitution

Let $x = e^{-h\nu / k_B T}$. The sums become geometric series: $$\text{Denominator} = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \dots = \frac{1}{1-x}$$ $$\text{Numerator} = h\nu (0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot x + 2 \cdot x^2 + 3 \cdot x^3 + \dots)$$ The series inside the parenthesis is $x(1 + 2x + 3x^2 + \dots) = x \frac{d}{dx} (1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \dots) = x \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{1}{1-x} \right) = \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}$. $$\text{Numerator} = h\nu \cdot \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}$$

Step 4: Final Expression

Divide the numerator by the denominator: $$\langle E \rangle = \frac{h\nu \frac{x}{(1-x)^2}}{\frac{1}{1-x}} = \frac{h\nu x}{1-x}$$ Substitute $x = e^{-h\nu / k_B T}$ back: $$\langle E \rangle = \frac{h\nu e^{-h\nu / k_B T}}{1 - e^{-h\nu / k_B T}}$$ Divide the numerator and denominator by $e^{-h\nu / k_B T}$: $$\mathbf{\langle E \rangle = \frac{h\nu}{e^{h\nu / k_B T} - 1}}$$ This is the correct quantum expression for the average energy of a Planck oscillator, which is fundamental to the Planck Radiation Formula.

Q.B7. Explain the concept of ensemble. Differentiate between microcanonical and grand canonical ensembles. Where are they used?

Detailed Explanation: Ensembles (The Statistical Toolbox)

1. Concept of Ensemble

In Statistical Mechanics, measuring the properties of a single system over a long period of time (a time average) is impractical. The Ensemble Concept replaces this with an ensemble average. > An ensemble is an infinite (or very large) collection of macroscopically identical, independent systems that all represent the same thermodynamic state as the single system being studied. * Macroscopically Identical: All systems have the same values for observable parameters like $P, V, T$. * Independent: Each system in the collection exists in its own microstate without influencing the others. The average value of a property taken over all the systems in the ensemble is assumed to be equal to the average value of that property measured over time for the single, actual system (**Ergodic Hypothesis**).

2. Differentiation: Microcanonical vs. Grand Canonical

The different types of ensembles are defined by the macroscopic parameters they hold constant and the types of exchange (energy, particles, volume) they allow with the surroundings (the "reservoir"). | Feature | Microcanonical Ensemble (MCE) | Grand Canonical Ensemble (GCE) | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | Constant Parameters | $E, V, N$ (Energy, Volume, Number of Particles) | $T, V, \mu$ (Temperature, Volume, Chemical Potential) | | Nature of Systems | Isolated (Cannot exchange energy or particles) | Open (Can exchange energy and particles) | | Fluctuating Quantity | None. State is fixed by $E, V, N$. | $E$ and $N$ (Energy and particle number can fluctuate) | | Associated Potential | Entropy ($S$) | Grand Potential ($\Omega_G$) |

3. Usage of Ensembles

| Ensemble | When is it Used? | | :--- | :--- | | Microcanonical (MCE) | Theoretically used for systems where the total Energy is fixed, such as deriving the foundational relations of statistical mechanics ($S=k_B \ln \Omega$). It is the most fundamental ensemble. | | Grand Canonical (GCE) | Used for systems that exchange both heat and particles with a large reservoir. Excellent for studying complex systems like chemical reactions, adsorption of gases on a surface, or the behavior of electrons in a semiconductor (where particle number effectively changes). | | Canonical Ensemble (CE) | (For context): Constant $T, V, N$. Used for closed systems (fixed $N$) in contact with a heat bath (fixed $T$). Ideal for most laboratory experiments. |

Q.B8. What is Bose–Einstein Statistics? State its basic postulates and derive the Bose–Einstein Distribution Law.

Detailed Explanation: Bose–Einstein Statistics and Distribution Law Bose–Einstein (B-E) Statistics is one of the two major quantum statistics (the other is Fermi-Dirac) and is essential for understanding phenomena like black-body radiation and Bose–Einstein Condensation.

1. What is Bose–Einstein Statistics?

B-E Statistics applies to a class of particles called Bosons. * Bosons: Particles with integer spin ($s = 0, 1, 2, \dots$). Examples include photons (spin 1), phonons, and helium-4 atoms (spin 0).

2. Basic Postulates

Postulate 1: Indistinguishability

> All bosons are completely identical and indistinguishable. Interchanging two particles in a system does not lead to a new, distinct microstate.

Postulate 2: No Restriction on Occupation (Pauli Exclusion Principle Violation)

> There is no restriction on the number of bosons that can occupy a given single-particle quantum state. Any number of bosons (from zero to infinity) can crowd into the same energy level.

3. Derivation of the Bose–Einstein Distribution Law

The goal is to find the most probable distribution of $N$ indistinguishable bosons among energy levels $\epsilon_i$.

Step 1: Thermodynamic Probability ($\Omega_{BE}$)

The number of ways $\Omega_i$ to distribute $n_i$ indistinguishable particles into $g_i$ (degeneracy) quantum states of energy $\epsilon_i$ is given by: $$\Omega_i = \frac{(n_i + g_i - 1)!}{n_i! (g_i - 1)!}$$ The total thermodynamic probability is the product over all energy levels: $$\Omega_{BE} = \prod_i \Omega_i$$

Step 2: Maximization (Lagrange Multipliers)

We maximize $\ln \Omega_{BE}$ subject to two constraints (constant total number of particles $N$ and constant total energy $E$) using the method of Lagrange Multipliers ($\alpha$ and $\beta$): 1. $\sum n_i = N \quad (\text{Constraint 1})$ 2. $\sum n_i \epsilon_i = E \quad (\text{Constraint 2})$ 3. $\delta (\ln \Omega_{BE} + \alpha N + \beta E) = 0 \quad (\text{Condition for maximum})$

Step 3: Final Result

Solving the maximization leads to the average number of particles $\langle n_i \rangle$ (the occupancy) for the $i$-th energy state: $$\mathbf{\langle n_i \rangle = \frac{1}{e^{\alpha + \beta \epsilon_i} - 1}}$$ By comparing this to the thermodynamic identity for the Grand Canonical Ensemble, the Lagrange multipliers are identified: $$\beta = 1/k_B T \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha = -\mu/k_B T$$ Final Bose–Einstein Distribution Law: The average number of bosons occupying the state with energy $\epsilon_i$ is: $$\mathbf{\langle n_i \rangle = \frac{1}{e^{(\epsilon_i - \mu)/k_B T} - 1}}$$ This formula is essential for describing phenomena like Bose–Einstein Condensation (BEC) and the radiation spectrum of photons.
