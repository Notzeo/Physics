Q1. Long Numerical (First Law & Isobaric Process): A gas sample containing \(2.0 \text{ moles}\) is heated at a constant pressure of \(1.5 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}\) from an initial volume of \(50 \text{ L}\) to a final volume of \(100 \text{ L}\). If the internal energy change is \(\Delta U = 12,000 \text{ J}\), calculate the total heat (\(\Delta Q\)) absorbed by the gas in $\text{kJ}$. (\(1 \text{ L} = 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3\))

A. \(19.5 \text{ kJ}\)
B. \(4.5 \text{ kJ}\)
C. \(19.5 \text{ kJ}\)
D. \(15.0 \text{ kJ}\)
Solution (Q1): Calculation of Heat Absorbed ($\Delta Q$)

1. Calculate the work done (\(W\)) by the gas during the isobaric expansion: $$W = P \Delta V = P (V_f - V_i)$$ $$W = (1.5 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}) \times (100 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3 - 50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3)$$ $$W = (1.5 \times 10^5) \times (0.05 \text{ m}^3) = 7,500 \text{ J}$$

2. Apply the First Law of Thermodynamics (\(\Delta Q = \Delta U + W\)): $$\Delta Q = 12,000 \text{ J} + 7,500 \text{ J} = 19,500 \text{ J}$$

3. Convert to kilojoules: $$\Delta Q = 19.5 \text{ kJ}$$

Final Answer: \(\mathbf{19.5 \text{ kJ}}\) (Option C).

2. Calculate the work done (\(W\)) for an adiabatic process (\(W = \frac{P_1 V_1 - P_2 V_2}{\gamma - 1}\)): $$W = \frac{(5 \times 20) - (1.435 \times 50)}{1.4 - 1} \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm}$$ $$W = \frac{100 - 71.75}{0.4} \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm} = \frac{28.25}{0.4} \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm} = 70.625 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm}$$

3. Convert work to Joules: $$W = 70.625 \times 101.3 \text{ J} \approx 7155.16 \text{ J}$$

Q3. Numerical (Van der Waals Work): Calculate the work done by $1.0 \text{ mole}$ of a gas in an isothermal expansion at $300 \text{ K}$ from $V_1$ to $V_2$ if the gas obeys the Van der Waals equation of state. Given $V_1 = 1 \text{ L}$, $V_2 = 5 \text{ L}$, and Van der Waals constants $a = 0.55 \text{ Pa}\cdot\text{m}^6/\text{mol}^2$ and $b = 3.0 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^3/\text{mol}$.

Solution (Q3): Isothermal Work for Van der Waals Gas

1. The differential work done is $dW = P dV$. From the VDW equation $P = \frac{R T}{V-b} - \frac{a}{V^2}$. $$W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P dV = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \left( \frac{R T}{V-b} - \frac{a}{V^2} \right) dV$$

2. Integrate the expression: $$W = R T \ln \left( \frac{V_2 - b}{V_1 - b} \right) + a \left( \frac{1}{V_2} - \frac{1}{V_1} \right)$$

3. Convert volumes to $\text{m}^3$: $V_1 = 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$, $V_2 = 5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$. Use $R \approx 8.314 \text{ J/mol}\cdot\text{K}$.

4. Calculate the first term (Ideal Gas like): $$\text{Term}_1 \approx (8.314)(300) \ln \left( \frac{5 \times 10^{-3} - 3 \times 10^{-5}}{1 \times 10^{-3} - 3 \times 10^{-5}} \right) \approx 2494.2 \ln (5.05) \approx 4040.5 \text{ J}$$

5. Calculate the second term (Correction term): $$\text{Term}_2 = 0.55 \left( \frac{1}{5 \times 10^{-3}} - \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} \right) = 0.55 \left( 200 - 1000 \right) = 0.55 \times (-800) = -440 \text{ J}$$

6. Total Work: $$W = 4040.5 \text{ J} - 440 \text{ J} = 3600.5 \text{ J}$$

Final Answer: \(\mathbf{W \approx 3600.5 \text{ J}}\).

Q4. Conceptual (Joule-Thomson): The temperature of a real gas may increase or decrease during a Joule-Thomson expansion. Which condition dictates the cooling effect (\(\mu_{JT} > 0\))?

A. The initial temperature is below the critical temperature.
B. The internal energy $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T$ is negative.
C. The initial pressure is less than the maximum inversion pressure.
D. The initial temperature is below the inversion temperature $\mathbf{T_i}$.
Solution (Q4): Joule-Thomson Coefficient

The Joule-Thomson coefficient is defined as \(\mu_{JT} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H\).

Cooling occurs when \(\mu_{JT} > 0\), meaning \(dT\) is negative when $dP$ is negative (expansion). This condition is met when the initial temperature of the gas is below its inversion temperature ($T_i$). Above $T_i$, heating occurs (\(\mu_{JT} < 0\)).

The correct condition is: $\mathbf{The \ initial \ temperature \ is \ below \ the \ inversion \ temperature \ T_i}$. (Option D).

Q5. Numerical (Heat Capacities): The difference between the molar heat capacities of a gas is given by $C_P - C_V = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$. Use this relation to prove $C_P - C_V = R$ for an ideal gas that satisfies $P V = R T$.

Solution (Q5): Proving $C_P - C_V = R$

1. Start with the ideal gas equation: \(P V = R T\).

2. Calculate the first partial derivative $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$ (Differentiating with respect to $T$ at constant $V$): $$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (P V)\right)_V = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (R T)\right)_V$$ $$V \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = R \implies \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{R}{V}$$

3. Calculate the second partial derivative $\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$ (Differentiating with respect to $T$ at constant $P$): $$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (P V)\right)_P = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (R T)\right)_P$$ $$P \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = R \implies \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = \frac{R}{P}$$

4. Substitute these results into the given relation $C_P - C_V = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$: $$C_P - C_V = T \left( \frac{R}{V} \right) \left( \frac{R}{P} \right) = \frac{R^2 T}{P V}$$

5. Substitute $P V = R T$ from the ideal gas law: $$C_P - C_V = \frac{R^2 T}{R T} = R$$

Final Answer: $C_P - C_V = R$ is proven for an ideal gas.

Q6. Numerical (Quasi-static Work/Integration): $2 \text{ moles}$ of an ideal gas undergo a quasi-static expansion defined by the path $P V^2 = \text{constant}$. If the initial state is $P_1 = 2 \text{ atm}, V_1 = 10 \text{ L}$ and the final volume is $V_2 = 40 \text{ L}$, calculate the work done $W$. (Use $1 \text{ L} \cdot \text{atm} = 101.3 \text{ J}$).

Solution (Q6): Work Done for Polytropic Process $P V^2 = C$

1. The work done is given by: $W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P dV$. For $P V^2 = C$, we have $P = C/V^2$. $$W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{C}{V^2} dV = C \left[ -\frac{1}{V} \right]_{V_1}^{V_2} = C \left( \frac{1}{V_1} - \frac{1}{V_2} \right)$$

2. Find the constant $C$: $C = P_1 V_1^2 = 2 \text{ atm} \times (10 \text{ L})^2 = 200 \text{ L}^2 \cdot \text{atm}$.

3. Calculate the work in $\text{L}\cdot\text{atm}$: $$W = 200 \left( \frac{1}{10 \text{ L}} - \frac{1}{40 \text{ L}} \right) = 200 \left( \frac{4-1}{40} \right) = 200 \times \frac{3}{40} = 5 \times 3 = 15 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm}$$

4. Convert to Joules: $$W = 15 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm} \times 101.3 \text{ J/L}\cdot\text{atm} = 1519.5 \text{ J}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{W = 1519.5 \text{ J}}$.

Q9. Numerical (Distinction between $\mu_{JT}$ and Adiabatic): Calculate the change in temperature when air ($\gamma=1.4$) at $300 \text{ K}$ is compressed adiabatically to 4 times its original pressure. Compare this result to the $\Delta T$ if the same compression occurs under a Joule-Thomson process ($\mu_{JT} \approx 0.1 \text{ K/atm}$).

Solution (Q9): Adiabatic vs. Joule-Thomson Compression

Part 1: Adiabatic Compression

Use $T P^{(1-\gamma)/\gamma} = \text{constant}$: $$T_2 = T_1 \left( \frac{P_2}{P_1} \right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$$ $$\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma} = \frac{1.4 - 1}{1.4} = \frac{0.4}{1.4} \approx 0.2857$$ $$T_2 = 300 \text{ K} \times (4)^{0.2857} \approx 300 \text{ K} \times 1.569 = 470.7 \text{ K}$$ $$\Delta T_{\text{Adiabatic}} = 470.7 \text{ K} - 300 \text{ K} = 170.7 \text{ K}$$

Part 2: Joule-Thomson Process ($\Delta H = 0$)

The Joule-Thomson coefficient $\mu_{JT} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H$. $$\Delta T_{JT} \approx \mu_{JT} \Delta P$$ Assuming $P_1 = 1 \text{ atm}$, then $\Delta P = P_2 - P_1 = 4 \text{ atm} - 1 \text{ atm} = 3 \text{ atm}$. $$\Delta T_{JT} \approx 0.1 \text{ K/atm} \times 3 \text{ atm} = 0.3 \text{ K}$$

Comparison: Adiabatic compression results in a substantial heating ($\mathbf{170.7 \text{ K}}$ rise) because work is done *on* the system with no heat exchange. Joule-Thomson is an isenthalpic process; since $\mu_{JT} > 0$, the expansion would cause cooling, but the compression causes a very minor heating of $\mathbf{0.3 \text{ K}}$ (if $T_i < T_I$). The physical processes are fundamentally different ($\Delta Q=0$ vs $\Delta H=0$).

Solution (Q10): Zeroth Law

The Zeroth Law states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are also in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law establishes the concept of temperature as a state function, allowing for its measurement and comparison.

Q10b. Numerical (Distinction between $\mu_{JT}$ and Joule Expansion): For an ideal gas, calculate $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U$ (Joule expansion coefficient). Discuss the physical significance compared to the Joule-Thomson coefficient $\mu_{JT}$.

Solution (Q10b): Joule Expansion Coefficient ($\eta_J$)

1. The Joule expansion coefficient is $\eta_J = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U$.

2. Start from the expression for the change in internal energy $dU$ (First Law for Ideal Gas): $$d U = C_V d T + \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T d V$$

3. For an ideal gas, $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = 0$ (internal energy is independent of volume). Thus: $$d U = C_V d T$$

4. For a Joule expansion, $d U = 0$ (free expansion into a vacuum, $W=0, Q=0$). $$0 = C_V d T$$

5. Since $C_V$ is non-zero, $d T$ must be zero, or $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U = 0$. $$\eta_J = 0$$

Significance:

  • Joule Expansion ($\eta_J = 0$): In a free expansion ($\Delta U = 0$), the temperature change is zero for an ideal gas.
  • Joule-Thomson ($\mu_{JT} = 0$): In an isenthalpic expansion ($\Delta H = 0$), the temperature change is zero for an ideal gas.

Both coefficients are zero for an ideal gas, reflecting that internal energy and enthalpy are independent of volume/pressure for an ideal gas.

Q11. Long Numerical (Carnot Cycle and Work): A Carnot engine operating between $T_1 = 500 \text{ K}$ and $T_2 = 300 \text{ K}$ performs $1000 \text{ J}$ of work per cycle. Calculate the heat absorbed from the source ($Q_1$) and the heat rejected to the sink ($Q_2$).

Solution (Q11): Carnot Cycle Heat Calculation

1. Calculate the efficiency $\eta$: $$\eta = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 1 - \frac{300 \text{ K}}{500 \text{ K}} = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4$$

2. Use the definition of efficiency $\eta = \frac{W}{Q_1}$ to find $Q_1$: $$Q_1 = \frac{W}{\eta} = \frac{1000 \text{ J}}{0.4} = 2500 \text{ J}$$

3. Use the First Law for the cycle $W = Q_1 - Q_2$ to find $Q_2$: $$Q_2 = Q_1 - W = 2500 \text{ J} - 1000 \text{ J} = 1500 \text{ J}$$

4. Check (Alternative calculation for $Q_2$): For a Carnot engine, $\frac{Q_1}{T_1} = \frac{Q_2}{T_2}$. $$Q_2 = Q_1 \left( \frac{T_2}{T_1} \right) = 2500 \text{ J} \times \frac{300}{500} = 2500 \text{ J} \times 0.6 = 1500 \text{ J}$$

Final Answer: Heat absorbed $\mathbf{Q_1 = 2500 \text{ J}}$, Heat rejected $\mathbf{Q_2 = 1500 \text{ J}}$.

Q16. Numerical (Clausius Inequality): A cyclic process absorbs $1000 \text{ J}$ of heat at $400 \text{ K}$ and rejects $600 \text{ J}$ of heat at $300 \text{ K}$. Calculate the total entropy change of the universe and determine if the process is reversible, irreversible, or impossible.

Solution (Q16): Clausius Inequality

1. Calculate the entropy change of the system (engine) over one cycle: $\Delta S_{\text{system}} = 0$ (cyclic process).

2. Calculate the entropy change of the surroundings: $$\Delta S_{\text{surr}} = \Delta S_{\text{source}} + \Delta S_{\text{sink}}$$

3. Entropy change of the source ($Q_1$ leaving, so $\Delta Q_{\text{source}} = -1000 \text{ J}$): $$\Delta S_{\text{source}} = \frac{-1000 \text{ J}}{400 \text{ K}} = -2.5 \text{ J/K}$$

4. Entropy change of the sink ($Q_2$ entering, so $\Delta Q_{\text{sink}} = +600 \text{ J}$): $$\Delta S_{\text{sink}} = \frac{+600 \text{ J}}{300 \text{ K}} = +2.0 \text{ J/K}$$

5. Total entropy change of the universe: $$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} = 0 + (-2.5 \text{ J/K} + 2.0 \text{ J/K}) = -0.5 \text{ J/K}$$

Conclusion: Since $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} < 0$, the process is impossible according to the Second Law ($\Delta S_{\text{universe}} \ge 0$).

Final Answer: $\mathbf{\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = -0.5 \text{ J/K}}$, Process is Impossible.

Q21. Derivation/Application (Maxwell's Equation): Starting from the Helmholtz Free Energy, $F = U - T S$, derive the corresponding Maxwell's thermodynamic relation: $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$.

Solution (Q21): Maxwell's Relation from Helmholtz Free Energy

1. Write the differential of Helmholtz Free Energy $F$: $$d F = d U - T d S - S d T$$

2. Substitute the combined First and Second Law ($d U = T d S - P d V$): $$d F = (T d S - P d V) - T d S - S d T$$ $$d F = - S d T - P d V$$

3. From the exactness of $d F$, since $F$ is a state function, we can write: $$d F = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_V d T + \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T d V$$

4. Comparing the coefficients from steps 2 and 3: $$\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_V = - S \quad \text{and} \quad \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T = - P$$

5. Apply the cross-differentiation property (Clairaut's theorem) $\frac{\partial}{\partial V}\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial T}\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)$: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial V} (- S) = \frac{\partial}{\partial T} (- P)$$ $$-\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$$

6. Simplify to get the final relation: $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V}$$

Q46. Numerical (Microstates in Phase Space): Estimate the minimum size of a single cell ($h_0$) in the phase space of a single particle, based on the uncertainty principle.

Solution (Q46): Phase Space Cell Size

1. According to the uncertainty principle, the minimum uncertainty in measuring a pair of canonical conjugate variables (like position $x$ and momentum $p_x$) is $\Delta x \Delta p_x \ge h$, where $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$).

2. A cell in 6D $\mu$-space has a volume of $d \Gamma = d x d y d z d p_x d p_y d p_z$.

3. The minimum volume of a single cell ($h_0$) is the product of the minimum uncertainties for each pair of variables: $$h_0 = (\Delta x \Delta p_x) (\Delta y \Delta p_y) (\Delta z \Delta p_z) = h^3$$

Final Answer: The minimum size of a phase space cell is $\mathbf{h_0 = h^3}$ (where $h$ is Planck's constant).

Q47. Comparison (Classical vs. Quantum Statistics): Explain the fundamental difference between Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) statistics and Bose-Einstein (BE) or Fermi-Dirac (FD) statistics regarding the nature of the particles and their occupancy of quantum states.

Solution (Q47): Comparison of Statistics
  • Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB - Classical):
    • Nature: Particles are considered distinguishable (can be labeled).
    • Occupancy: There is no restriction on the number of particles that can occupy a single quantum state.
  • Bose-Einstein (BE - Quantum):
    • Nature: Particles are considered indistinguishable (Bosons, integer spin).
    • Occupancy: No restriction on occupancy (any number of Bosons can occupy one state).
  • Fermi-Dirac (FD - Quantum):
    • Nature: Particles are considered indistinguishable (Fermions, half-integer spin).
    • Occupancy: Pauli Exclusion Principle applies: only one Fermion can occupy a single quantum state.
