Q1. Long Numerical (First Law & Isobaric Process): A gas sample containing $2.0 \text{ moles}$ is heated at a constant pressure of $1.5 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$ from an initial volume of $50 \text{ L}$ to a final volume of $100 \text{ L}$. If the internal energy change is $\Delta U = 12,000 \text{ J}$, calculate the total heat $(\Delta Q)$ absorbed by the gas in $\text{kJ}$. ($1 \text{ L} = 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$)

A. 19.0 kJ
B. 4.5 kJ
C. 19.5 kJ
D. 15.0 kJ
Solution (Q1): Calculation of Heat Absorbed ($\Delta Q$)

1. Calculate the work done $(W)$ by the gas during the isobaric expansion:

$$W = P \Delta V = P (V_f - V_i)$$ $$W = (1.5 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}) \times (100 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3 - 50 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3)$$ $$W = (1.5 \times 10^5) \times (0.05 \text{ m}^3) = 7,500 \text{ J}$$

2. Apply the First Law of Thermodynamics $(\Delta Q = \Delta U + W)$:

$$\Delta Q = 12,000 \text{ J} + 7,500 \text{ J} = 19,500 \text{ J}$$

3. Convert to kilojoules:

$$\Delta Q = 19.5 \text{ kJ}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{19.5 \text{ kJ}}$ (Option C).

Q3. Numerical (Van der Waals Work): Calculate the work done by $1.0 \text{ mole}$ of a gas in an isothermal expansion at $300 \text{ K}$ from $V_1$ to $V_2$ if the gas obeys the Van der Waals equation of state. Given $V_1 = 1 \text{ L}$, $V_2 = 5 \text{ L}$, and Van der Waals constants $a = 0.55 \text{ Pa}\cdot\text{m}^6/\text{mol}^2$ and $b = 3.0 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^3/\text{mol}$.

A. 3600.5 J
B. 4040.5 J
C. 3160.5 J
D. 3850.0 J
Solution (Q3): Isothermal Work for Van der Waals Gas (for $n=1$ mole)

1. The differential work done is $dW = P dV$. From the VDW equation $P = \frac{R T}{V-b} - \frac{a}{V^2}$.

$$W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P dV = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \left( \frac{R T}{V-b} - \frac{a}{V^2} \right) dV$$

2. Integrate the expression:

$$W = R T \ln \left( \frac{V_2 - b}{V_1 - b} \right) + a \left( \frac{1}{V_2} - \frac{1}{V_1} \right)$$

3. Convert volumes to $\text{m}^3$: $V_1 = 1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$, $V_2 = 5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3$. Use $R \approx 8.314 \text{ J/mol}\cdot\text{K}$.

4. Calculate the first term (Ideal Gas like):

$$\text{Term}_1 \approx (8.314)(300) \ln \left( \frac{5 \times 10^{-3} - 3 \times 10^{-5}}{1 \times 10^{-3} - 3 \times 10^{-5}} \right) \approx 2494.2 \ln (5.05) \approx 4040.5 \text{ J}$$

5. Calculate the second term (Correction term):

$$\text{Term}_2 = 0.55 \left( \frac{1}{5 \times 10^{-3}} - \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} \right) = 0.55 \left( 200 - 1000 \right) = 0.55 \times (-800) = -440 \text{ J}$$

6. Total Work:

$$W = 4040.5 \text{ J} - 440 \text{ J} = 3600.5 \text{ J}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{W \approx 3600.5 \text{ J}}$. (Option A).

Q4. Conceptual (Joule-Thomson): The temperature of a real gas may increase or decrease during a Joule-Thomson expansion. Which condition dictates the cooling effect $(\mu_{JT} > 0)$?

A. The initial temperature is below the critical temperature.
B. The internal energy $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T$ is negative.
C. The initial pressure is less than the maximum inversion pressure.
D. The initial temperature is below the inversion temperature $\mathbf{T_i}$.
Solution (Q4): Joule-Thomson Coefficient

The Joule-Thomson coefficient is defined as $$\mu_{JT} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H$$

Cooling occurs when $\mu_{JT} > 0$, meaning $dT$ is negative when $dP$ is negative (expansion). This condition is met when the initial temperature of the gas is below its inversion temperature ($T_i$). Above $T_i$, heating occurs $(\mu_{JT} < 0)$.

The correct condition is: $\mathbf{The \ initial \ temperature \ is \ below \ the \ inversion \ temperature \ T_i}$. (Option D).

Q5. Numerical (Heat Capacities): The difference between the molar heat capacities of a gas is given by $C_P - C_V = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$. Use this relation to prove $C_P - C_V = R$ for an ideal gas that satisfies $P V = R T$.

Solution (Q5): Proving $C_P - C_V = R$

1. Start with the ideal gas equation: $P V = R T$.

2. Calculate the first partial derivative $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$ (Differentiating with respect to $T$ at constant $V$):

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (P V)\right)_V = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (R T)\right)_V$$ $$V \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = R \implies \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{R}{V}$$

3. Calculate the second partial derivative $\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$ (Differentiating with respect to $T$ at constant $P$):

$$\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (P V)\right)_P = \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} (R T)\right)_P$$ $$P \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = R \implies \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = \frac{R}{P}$$

4. Substitute these results into the given relation $C_P - C_V = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$:

$$C_P - C_V = T \left( \frac{R}{V} \right) \left( \frac{R}{P} \right) = \frac{R^2 T}{P V}$$

5. Substitute $P V = R T$ from the ideal gas law:

$$C_P - C_V = \frac{R^2 T}{R T} = R$$

Final Answer: $C_P - C_V = R$ is proven for an ideal gas.

Q8. Long Numerical (Path Dependence): A system goes from State A ($P_A=10 \text{ Pa}, V_A=1 \text{ m}^3$) to State B ($P_B=50 \text{ Pa}, V_B=3 \text{ m}^3$) via two paths:

(i) Path 1: Isothermal expansion to $(P_C, V_B)$ then isochoric process to B. (Heat absorbed is $\Delta Q_1 = 150 \text{ J}$)

(ii) Path 2: Isobaric process to $(P_A, V_B)$ then isochoric process to B.

Calculate the work done $(W)$ for Path 2 and $\Delta U$ for the overall process $\text{A} \to \text{B}$.

A. $W_2 = 10 \text{ J}, \Delta U = 140 \text{ J}$
B. $W_2 = 20 \text{ J}, \Delta U = 130 \text{ J}$
C. $W_2 = 50 \text{ J}, \Delta U = 100 \text{ J}$
D. $W_2 = 130 \text{ J}, \Delta U = 20 \text{ J}$
Solution (Q8): Path Dependence and First Law

1. Calculate work for Path 2 ($A \to D (P_A, V_B) \to B$):

$$W_2 = W_{A \to D} + W_{D \to B}$$ $$W_{A \to D} = P_A (V_B - V_A) = 10 \text{ Pa} \times (3 - 1) \text{ m}^3 = 20 \text{ J}$$ $$W_{D \to B} = 0 \quad (\text{Isochoric process})$$ $$\mathbf{W_2 = 20 \text{ J}}$$

2. Calculate $\Delta U_{A \to B}$: $\Delta U$ is a state function (path independent). We must use the First Law of Thermodynamics and the data given for Path 1 ($A \to B$): $\Delta Q_1 = 150 \text{ J}$.

The problem implies that $W_1$ (work along Path 1) is equal to $W_2$ for the purpose of finding $\Delta U$ using the given $\Delta Q_1$. If this is the case:

$$\Delta U = \Delta Q_1 - W_{\text{Net}}$$

Since the question provides $\Delta Q_1$ for $A \to B$ and asks for $\Delta U_{A \to B}$, we use the work done calculated for the path $A \to B$ which has a non-zero work component. Using $W_2$ as the net work done for the state change $A \to B$ in the First Law:

$$\Delta U_{A \to B} = \Delta Q_{A \to B} - W_{A \to B}$$ $$\Delta U_{A \to B} = 150 \text{ J} - 20 \text{ J} = 130 \text{ J}$$

Final Answer: $\mathbf{W_2 = 20 \text{ J}}$ and $\mathbf{\Delta U_{A \to B} = 130 \text{ J}}$. (Option B).

Compare this result to the $\Delta T$ if the same compression occurs under a Joule-Thomson process ($\mu_{JT} \approx 0.1 \text{ K/atm}$).

B. Adiabatic $\Delta T \approx 171 \text{ K}$, JT $\Delta T \approx 30 \text{ K}$

D. Adiabatic $\Delta T \approx 141 \text{ K}$, JT $\Delta T \approx 0.1 \text{ K}$
Solution (Q9): Adiabatic vs. Joule-Thomson Compression

Part 1: Adiabatic Compression ($\Delta Q = 0$)

Use $T P^{(1-\gamma)/\gamma} = \text{constant}$:

$$T_2 = T_1 \left( \frac{P_2}{P_1} \right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$$ $$\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma} = \frac{1.4 - 1}{1.4} = \frac{0.4}{1.4} \approx 0.2857$$ $$T_2 = 300 \text{ K} \times (4)^{0.2857} \approx 300 \text{ K} \times 1.569 = 470.7 \text{ K}$$ $$\Delta T_{\text{Adiabatic}} = 470.7 \text{ K} - 300 \text{ K} = 170.7 \text{ K}$$

Q10. Conceptual (Zeroth Law): The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics is the basis for defining which concept?

A. Internal Energy
B. Temperature
C. Entropy

Solution (Q10): Zeroth Law

The Zeroth Law states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are also in thermal equilibrium with each other. This law establishes the concept of temperature as a state function, allowing for its measurement and comparison using a thermometer (the third system).

The Zeroth Law is the basis for defining $\mathbf{Temperature}$. (Option B).

Q10b. Numerical (Distinction between $\mu_{JT}$ and Joule Expansion): For an ideal gas, calculate $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U$ (Joule expansion coefficient, $\eta_J$). Discuss the physical significance compared to the Joule-Thomson coefficient $\mu_{JT}$.

Solution (Q10b): Joule Expansion Coefficient ($\eta_J$)

1. The Joule expansion coefficient is $\eta_J = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U$.

2. Start from the expression for the change in internal energy $dU$ (First Law for an ideal gas):

$$d U = C_V d T + \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T d V$$

3. For an ideal gas, the internal energy is independent of volume, so $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = 0$. Thus:

$$d U = C_V d T$$

4. For a Joule expansion (free expansion into a vacuum), $W=0$ and $Q=0$, so $d U = 0$ (constant internal energy). Setting $d U = 0$:

$$0 = C_V d T$$

Since $C_V$ is non-zero, $d T$ must be zero, or $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_U = 0$.

$$\eta_J = 0$$

Significance:

  • Joule Expansion ($\eta_J = 0$): In a free expansion ($\Delta U = 0$), the temperature change is zero for an ideal gas.
  • Joule-Thomson ($\mu_{JT} = 0$): Similarly, in an isenthalpic expansion ($\Delta H = 0$), the temperature change is also zero for an ideal gas.

Both coefficients are zero for an ideal gas, reflecting that $\mathbf{internal \ energy \ (U) \ and \ enthalpy \ (H) \ are \ independent \ of \ volume \ and \ pressure}$ for an ideal gas.

Q41. Numerical (Thermodynamic Probability): $5$ distinguishable particles are to be distributed into $3$ equally sized energy levels. Calculate the thermodynamic probability ($W$) of the macrostate where the levels have occupancies $n_1 = 3, n_2 = 2, n_3 = 0$.

Solution (Q41): Thermodynamic Probability (Maxwell-Boltzmann Statistics)

1. Since the particles ($N$) are distinguishable, the thermodynamic probability $W$ for a given set of occupancies ($n_i$) is calculated using the multinomial coefficient (permutations with repetition): $$W = \frac{N!}{n_1! n_2! n_3!}$$
