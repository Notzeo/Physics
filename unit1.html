Unit 1: Basic Concepts and First Law of Thermodynamics

B.Sc. Physics Essentials

1. Thermodynamic Systems üß™

1.1 Definition of System, Surroundings, and Boundary

  • Thermodynamic System: A defined quantity of matter or a region of space upon which attention is concentrated for thermodynamic analysis. It is the object of study.
  • Surroundings: Everything external to the system that can interact with the system (exchange energy or matter).
  • Boundary: The real or imaginary surface separating the system from its surroundings.
    • Fixed Boundary (e.g., rigid container walls).
    • Moving Boundary (e.g., piston in a cylinder).
    • Diathermal Boundary (Allows heat transfer).
    • Adiabatic Boundary (Does not allow heat transfer - insulated).

1.2 Types of Systems

System Type Exchange of Matter Exchange of Energy (Heat/Work) Example
Open System Yes Yes Water boiling in an open pot.
Closed System No Yes Gas sealed in a cylinder with a movable piston.
Isolated System No No A perfectly insulated thermos flask (ideal case).

Control Volume: A fixed region in space where matter flows in and out (used for open systems).

Control Mass: A specific, fixed quantity of matter (used for closed systems).

1.3 Properties of a System

  • Intensive Properties: Independent of the mass or size of the system.
    Examples: Temperature ($\text{T}$), Pressure ($\text{P}$), Density ($\rho$), Viscosity.
  • Extensive Properties: Dependent on the mass or size of the system.
    Examples: Volume ($\text{V}$), Internal Energy ($\text{U}$), Mass ($\text{m}$), Enthalpy ($\text{H}$).
  • Specific Properties: Extensive properties per unit mass, making them **intensive**.
    Examples: Specific Volume ($\text{v} = \text{V}/\text{m}$), Specific Internal Energy ($\text{u} = \text{U}/\text{m}$).

Quick Check Questions (1)

  1. A well-sealed, rigid (fixed volume) container of gas is heated. What type of thermodynamic system is this?
    See Answer
    Answer: A **Closed System**. It can exchange energy (heat) but not matter.
  2. Is **Specific Internal Energy** an extensive or intensive property? Why?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Intensive**. Although Internal Energy (U) is extensive, dividing it by mass (making it "specific") removes the dependence on the size of the system.
  3. If the boundary of a system is adiabatic, what type of exchange is prevented between the system and surroundings?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Heat exchange** ($\Delta Q=0$) is prevented.

2. Thermal Equilibrium and Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics üå°Ô∏è

2.2 Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

Statement: "If two thermodynamic systems, $\text{A}$ and $\text{B}$, are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third thermodynamic system, $\text{C}$, then $\text{A}$ and $\text{B}$ are also in thermal equilibrium with each other."

Role in Defining Temperature: It provides the logical basis for the concept of **Temperature** ($\text{T}$) as a measurable property ($\text{C}$ acts as a thermometer).

2.3 Temperature Scales

  • Absolute (Kelvin) Scale ($\text{K}$): $\text{0 K}$ is absolute zero.
  • Conversion: $$\text{T}(\text{K}) = \text{T}(^\circ \text{C}) + 273.15$$
Example Question: System $\text{A}$ and $\text{B}$ both reach the same final temperature as a table ($\text{C}$). What is the conclusion?

Answer: The Zeroth Law concludes that $\text{A}$ and $\text{B}$ are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

Quick Check Questions (2)

  1. What must be equal between two systems for them to be in **thermal equilibrium**?
    See Answer
    Answer: Their **temperatures** must be equal.
  2. If System A has a higher temperature than System B, which way will heat naturally flow when they are placed in contact?
    See Answer
    Answer: From **System A to System B** (from higher temperature to lower temperature).
  3. The Zeroth Law allows us to use what common device?
    See Answer
    Answer: A **thermometer**. The thermometer ($\text{C}$) is in equilibrium with the object ($\text{A}$) and establishes a common property (temperature) that can be compared to other objects ($\text{B}$).
---

3. Equation of State üî¢

3.1 Definition and Examples

Definition: An equation relating the state variables ($\text{P, V, T}$) of a system: $f(\text{P}, \text{V}, \text{T}) = 0$

  • Ideal Gas Equation: $$\text{PV} = \text{nRT}$$
  • Van der Waals Equation (Real Gas): $$(\text{P} + \text{a}/\text{V}^2)(\text{V} - \text{b}) = \text{RT} \quad \text{(per mole)}$$

3.4 Compressibility Factor ($\text{Z}$)

Quantifies the deviation from ideal behavior:

$$Z = \frac{\text{PV}}{\text{nRT}}$$
  • Ideal Gas: $\text{Z} = 1$.
  • Real Gas: $\text{Z} < 1$ (attractive forces dominate), $\text{Z} > 1$ (repulsive forces/volume dominate).

Quick Check Questions (3)

  1. $1 \text{ mole}$ of an ideal gas is at $1 \text{ atm}$ and $273.15 \text{ K}$. What is the value of its Compressibility Factor ($\text{Z}$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: **$\text{Z} = 1$**. By definition, the compressibility factor for an ideal gas is always 1.
  2. Under what two conditions do real gases typically behave most like an ideal gas?
    See Answer
    Answer: At **high temperatures** (reducing attractive forces) and **low pressures** (increasing distance between molecules).
  3. If a real gas has a compressibility factor $\text{Z} < 1$, what does this suggest about the intermolecular forces?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Attractive forces** between the molecules are dominant, making the gas more compressible than an ideal gas.
---

4. First Law of Thermodynamics ‚öñÔ∏è

4.1 Conservation of Energy

The First Law states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed (**conservation of energy**).

4.2 Mathematical Statement

Differential Form:

$$dQ = dU + dW$$

Integrated Form:

$$\Delta Q = \Delta U + \Delta W$$

We use the convention where $\Delta\text{W}$ is **work done by the system**.

4.4 Physical Meaning of Internal Energy ($\text{U}$)

  • $\text{U}$ is the total energy within the system (molecular $\text{KE} + \text{PE}$).
  • $\text{U}$ is a **State Function**: $\Delta\text{U}$ depends only on the initial and final states, not the path.
  • For an Ideal Gas: $\Delta\text{U} \propto \Delta\text{T}$.

Quick Check Questions (4)

  1. A system absorbs $100 \text{ J}$ of heat and performs $30 \text{ J}$ of work on the surroundings. What is the change in its internal energy ($\Delta U$)?
    See Answer
    Calculation: $\Delta U = \Delta Q - \Delta W = 100 \text{ J} - 30 \text{ J} = 70 \text{ J}$.

    Answer: **$70 \text{ J}$**.

  2. What is the change in internal energy ($\Delta U$) for a gas undergoing a complete thermodynamic cycle (returning to its initial state)?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Zero** ($\Delta U = 0$). Since internal energy (U) is a state function, its change is zero when the initial and final states are the same.
  3. According to the First Law, what must be true about the net heat absorbed ($\Delta Q$) and the net work done ($\Delta W$) during a cycle?
    See Answer
    Answer: They must be equal: **$\Delta Q = \Delta W$** (since $\Delta U = 0$).
---

5. Heat and Work ‚öôÔ∏è

5.1 Nature of Heat and Work as Path Functions

Work ($\text{W}$) and Heat ($\text{Q}$) are **Path Functions**. Their values depend on the specific path taken between states. Mathematically, $\text{dQ}$ and $\text{dW}$ are inexact differentials.

5.3 Work Done in Different Processes (Reversible)

  • Isothermal Expansion: $$\Delta W = \text{nRT} \ln\left(\frac{\text{V}_2}{\text{V}_1}\right)$$
  • Isobaric Process: $$\Delta W = \text{P}(\text{V}_2 - \text{V}_1)$$
  • Isochoric Process: $$\Delta W = 0$$
Graphical Representation:

The work done ($\Delta \text{W}$) in any process is equal to the **area under the curve** on a $\text{P‚ÄìV}$ diagram, visually illustrating path dependence.

Quick Check Questions (5)

  1. A gas expands from $V_1$ to $V_2$ at constant pressure $P$. Calculate the work done by the system.
    See Answer
    Answer: **$\Delta W = P(V_2 - V_1)$** (Isobaric work formula).
  2. If a gas is heated in a rigid container, how much work does it do on the surroundings?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Zero** ($\Delta W = 0$). This is an isochoric (constant volume) process, meaning no volume change occurs against the external pressure.
  3. Why are heat ($\Delta Q$) and work ($\Delta W$) considered path functions, while internal energy ($\Delta U$) is a state function?
    See Answer
    Answer: The amount of heat exchanged and work done **depends on the sequence of steps** (the path) taken between two states. $\Delta U$ only depends on the **initial and final values** of the system's properties.
---

6. Quasi-static, Reversible, and Irreversible Processes üîÑ

6.2 Reversible Process

Can be reversed by an infinitesimal change, restoring both system and surroundings to original states.

Conditions: Must be **quasi-static** (near equilibrium) and have **no dissipative forces** (friction, resistance).

6.3 Irreversible Process

All real-world processes are irreversible, caused by factors like **friction, free expansion,** or **heat transfer through finite $\Delta\text{T}$**.

Work is maximized in a reversible expansion ($\text{W}_{\text{irr}} < \text{W}_{\text{rev}}$).

Quick Check Questions (6)

  1. What is a **quasi-static** process?
    See Answer
    Answer: A process that occurs **infinitesimally slowly** such that the system is always very close to an equilibrium state throughout the entire process.
  2. Name one factor that makes an otherwise ideal thermodynamic process irreversible in the real world.
    See Answer
    Answer: **Friction** (or viscosity, electrical resistance, heat transfer across a finite temperature difference).
  3. Compare the work output of a reversible expansion versus an irreversible expansion between the same two states.
    See Answer
    Answer: The **reversible expansion does the maximum possible work** ($\text{W}_{\text{rev}} > \text{W}_{\text{irr}}$).

8. Heat Capacities üî•

8.2 Heat Capacities at Constant Volume ($\text{C}_{\text{V}}$) and Constant Pressure ($\text{C}_{\text{P}}$)

  • $\text{C}_{\text{V}}$ (Isochoric, $\Delta\text{W}=0$): Heat supplied goes only to internal energy. $$C_V = \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right)_V$$
  • $\text{C}_{\text{P}}$ (Isobaric): Heat supplied increases $\text{U}$ AND does work ($\Delta\text{W} \ne 0$). $$C_P = \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial T}\right)_P$$

8.3 Relationship between $\text{C}_{\text{P}}$ and $\text{C}_{\text{V}}$ (Mayer's Relation)

$$\mathbf{C}_{\mathbf{P}} - \mathbf{C}_{\mathbf{V}} = \mathbf{R} \quad \text{(For } 1 \text{ mole of ideal gas)}$$

Quick Check Questions (8)

  1. Why is the molar heat capacity at constant pressure ($\text{C}_{\text{P}}$) always greater than the molar heat capacity at constant volume ($\text{C}_{\text{V}}$) for an ideal gas?
    See Answer
    Answer: At constant pressure, the system does **work ($\Delta W$)** against the surroundings as it expands. This external work requires extra energy (heat) beyond the energy needed just to raise the internal energy ($\Delta U$).
  2. If the difference $C_P - C_V$ is found to be $8.314 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}$, what is this difference numerically equal to?
    See Answer
    Answer: It is equal to the **Universal Gas Constant ($\text{R}$)**, as stated by Mayer's Relation.
---

9. Adiabatic Processes üí®

9.1 Definition and Condition

Condition: No heat exchange ($\Delta\text{Q} = 0$).

First Law: $\Delta\text{Q} = \Delta\text{U} + \Delta\text{W} \implies \mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{U} = -\Delta\mathbf{W}}$.

Work is done at the expense of internal energy, leading to a temperature change.

9.2 Adiabatic Relation (Reversible)

$$\mathbf{\text{PV}^\gamma = \text{constant}}$$

Where $\gamma = \text{C}_{\text{P}}/\text{C}_{\text{V}}$ (Adiabatic index).

9.3 Other Relations

$$\text{T}\text{V}^{\gamma-1} = \text{constant}$$ $$\text{T}^\gamma \text{P}^{1-\gamma} = \text{constant}$$

9.4 Comparison: Isothermal vs. Adiabatic

  • The adiabatic curve is always **steeper** than the isothermal curve passing through the same point, since $\gamma > 1$.

Quick Check Questions (9)

  1. During a reversible adiabatic expansion, what happens to the temperature of the gas and why?
    See Answer
    Answer: The temperature **decreases**. Since $\Delta Q=0$, the gas does work ($\Delta W > 0$) at the expense of its internal energy ($\Delta U = -\Delta W < 0$), causing the temperature to drop.
  2. What is the significance of the **adiabatic index ($\gamma$)** in the equation $\text{PV}^\gamma = \text{constant}$?
    See Answer
    Answer: $\gamma$ is the ratio of heat capacities, $\mathbf{C_P / C_V}$. Its value determines the slope of the adiabatic curve on a P-V diagram.
  3. In a P-V diagram, why is the adiabatic curve steeper than the isothermal curve?
    See Answer
    Answer: Because the temperature changes (drops in expansion) during an adiabatic process, the pressure drops faster than in an isothermal process ($\text{P} \propto \text{V}^{-\gamma}$ with $\gamma > 1$, versus $\text{P} \propto \text{V}^{-1}$ for isothermal).
---

10. Van der Waals Equation (Real Gas) ü§Ø

10.2 Derivation and Corrections

$$(\mathbf{P} + \mathbf{a}/\mathbf{V}^{\mathbf{2}})(\mathbf{V} - \mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{RT} \quad \text{(per mole)}$$
  • **Pressure Correction ($\mathbf{a}/\mathbf{V}^{\mathbf{2}}$):** Accounts for intermolecular **attractive forces**.
  • **Volume Correction ($\mathbf{b}$):** Accounts for the **finite volume** occupied by the gas molecules.

10.4 Critical Constants

The critical point ($\text{P}_{\text{c}}, \text{V}_{\text{c}}, \text{T}_{\text{c}}$) marks where liquid and gas phases are indistinguishable.

$$V_{c} = 3b \quad P_{c} = \frac{a}{27b^{2}} \quad T_{c} = \frac{8a}{27Rb}$$

Quick Check Questions (10)

  1. Which term in the Van der Waals equation corrects the pressure term for attractive forces between gas molecules?
    See Answer
    Answer: The term **$a/V^2$**.
  2. The constant **$b$** in the Van der Waals equation is related to what physical property of the gas molecules?
    See Answer
    Answer: The **finite volume** (or size) of the molecules themselves.
  3. What happens to a gas when its temperature and pressure are at the critical point ($\text{T}_{\text{c}}, \text{P}_{\text{c}}$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: The liquid and gas phases become **indistinguishable**; it is impossible to liquefy the gas by pressure alone above $\text{T}_{\text{c}}$.
---

11. Joule, Joule‚ÄìThomson, and Adiabatic Expansions ü•∂

11.1 Joule‚Äôs Experiment (Free Expansion)

Conditions: $\mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{W} = 0}$ ($\text{P}_{\text{ext}} = 0$) and $\mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{Q} = 0}$.

Conclusion (Ideal Gas): $\mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{U} = 0}$ and thus $\mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{T} = 0}$.

11.2 Joule‚ÄìThomson Effect (Throttling Process)

Condition: Isenthalpic process ($\mathbf{\Delta\mathbf{H} = 0}$).

Coefficient: $$\mu_{\text{JT}} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H$$

  • $\mu_{\text{JT}} > 0$: **Cooling** (attractive forces dominate).
  • $\mu_{\text{JT}} < 0$: **Heating** (repulsive forces dominate).

Quick Check Questions (11)

  1. What is the key result regarding temperature change for an **ideal gas** during a free expansion (Joule's experiment)?
    See Answer
    Answer: The temperature change is **zero** ($\Delta T = 0$), because $\Delta U=0$ and the internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on temperature.
  2. The Joule‚ÄìThomson effect involves what constant thermodynamic quantity?
    See Answer
    Answer: The process is **Isenthalpic**, meaning the enthalpy ($\text{H}$) remains constant ($\Delta H = 0$).
  3. If a gas experiences cooling upon Joule‚ÄìThomson expansion, what does this imply about its $\mu_{\text{JT}}$ coefficient?
    See Answer
    Answer: $\mu_{\text{JT}}$ must be **positive** ($\mu_{\text{JT}} > 0$), as a drop in pressure ($\Delta P < 0$) resulted in a drop in temperature ($\Delta T < 0$).

12.3 Practice Numerical Problems (with Answers)

  1. $5 \text{ moles}$ of an ideal monatomic gas ($\text{C}_{\text{V}} = 3\text{R}/2$) are heated from $300 \text{ K}$ to $400 \text{ K}$ at constant pressure. Calculate $\Delta\text{Q}, \Delta\text{W}$, and $\Delta\text{U}$. ($\text{R} = 8.314 \text{ J K}^{-1} \text{ mol}^{-1}$)
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:

    Given: $n=5$, $\Delta T = 100 \text{ K}$. Constant pressure (Isobaric).

    1. **Calculate $\Delta U$**: $\Delta U = n C_V \Delta T$ (Always true for ideal gas).

    $$\Delta U = 5 \times (3/2 \times 8.314) \times 100 \approx \mathbf{6235.5 \text{ J}}$$

    2. **Calculate $\Delta Q$**: $\Delta Q = n C_P \Delta T$. Since $C_P = C_V + R = 5R/2$.

    $$\Delta Q = 5 \times (5/2 \times 8.314) \times 100 \approx \mathbf{10392.5 \text{ J}}$$

    3. **Calculate $\Delta W$**: Using the First Law, $\Delta W = \Delta Q - \Delta U$.

    $$\Delta W = 10392.5 \text{ J} - 6235.5 \text{ J} \approx \mathbf{4157.0 \text{ J}}$$
  2. $1 \text{ mole}$ of an ideal gas expands adiabatically from $2 \text{ atm}$ and $25 \text{ L}$ to $1 \text{ atm}$. If $\gamma = 1.4$, find the final volume and the work done. ($\text{R} = 8.314 \text{ J K}^{-1} \text{ mol}^{-1} \approx 0.082 \text{ L atm K}^{-1} \text{ mol}^{-1}$)
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:

    1. **Find $V_2$** (Final Volume): Use $P_1 V_1^\gamma = P_2 V_2^\gamma$.

    $$V_2 = V_1 \left(\frac{P_1}{P_2}\right)^{1/\gamma} = 25 \text{ L} \left(\frac{2 \text{ atm}}{1 \text{ atm}}\right)^{1/1.4} \approx 25 \text{ L} \times (2)^{0.714}$$ $$V_2 \approx 25 \text{ L} \times 1.65 \approx \mathbf{41.25 \text{ L}}$$

    2. **Find Work Done** ($\Delta W$): For adiabatic process, $\Delta W = -\Delta U = -n C_V \Delta T$. First, find $T_1$ and $T_2$ using $PV=nRT$. ($T_1 \approx 304.88 \text{ K}, T_2 \approx 251.52 \text{ K}$)

    Need $C_V$: $C_V = R/(\gamma-1) = 8.314 / 0.4 \approx 20.785 \text{ J/(mol}\cdot\text{K})$.

    $$\Delta W = -1 \times 20.785 \times (251.52 - 304.88) \approx \mathbf{1109.8 \text{ J}}$$

    Work done by the system is positive (expansion).

  3. $2 \text{ moles}$ of an ideal gas are compressed from $10 \text{ L}$ to $5 \text{ L}$ at a constant external pressure of $5 \text{ atm}$. Calculate the work done on the system in Joules. ($1 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm} \approx 101.3 \text{ J}$)
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:

    1. **Work Done BY the system** (Irreversible, constant $P_{\text{ext}}$): $\Delta W_{\text{by}} = P_{\text{ext}} (V_2 - V_1)$

    $$\Delta W_{\text{by}} = 5 \text{ atm} \times (5 \text{ L} - 10 \text{ L}) = -25 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm}$$

    2. **Work Done ON the system**:

    $$\Delta W_{\text{on}} = -\Delta W_{\text{by}} = +25 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm}$$

    3. **Convert to Joules**:

    $$\Delta W_{\text{on}} = 25 \text{ L}\cdot\text{atm} \times 101.3 \text{ J/(L}\cdot\text{atm)} \approx \mathbf{2532.5 \text{ J}}$$
