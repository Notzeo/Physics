Unit 2: Second Law of Thermodynamics and Entropy

Physics Essentials

1. Limitations of the First Law of Thermodynamics üö´

Recap of the First Law

The First Law is the Law of Conservation of Energy for thermodynamic systems:

$$\Delta Q = \Delta U + \Delta W$$

Why the First Law is Insufficient

The First Law only ensures energy balance; it cannot determine:

  • The direction of heat flow (e.g., hot to cold).
  • Whether a process is spontaneous (naturally occurring) or non-spontaneous.
  • The quality of energy: It suggests $100\%$ heat-to-work conversion is possible, which is false in a cyclic process.
Real-World Limitation: Heat always flows from higher temperature ($T_1$) to lower temperature ($T_2$). The reverse is forbidden by nature, though not by the First Law.

Quick Check Questions (1)

  1. If a process is allowed by the First Law ($\Delta Q = \Delta U + \Delta W$), does that mean it will occur spontaneously?
    See Answer
    Answer: No. The First Law only accounts for the quantity of energy transferred, not the direction or feasibility of the process (the domain of the Second Law).
  2. What key physical observation regarding heat flow is ignored by the First Law?
    See Answer
    Answer: The observation that heat naturally flows only from a hotter body to a colder body (not the reverse).
  3. The First Law permits an engine to convert $1000 \text{ J}$ of absorbed heat entirely into $1000 \text{ J}$ of work in a cycle. Why is this impossible in practice?
    See Answer
    Answer: This violates the Second Law of Thermodynamics (specifically, the Kelvin‚ÄìPlanck statement), which dictates that some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir.

2. Reversible and Irreversible Processes üîÑ

Definition of Reversible Process

A process where both the system and the surroundings can be restored exactly to their initial states without any net change in the universe.

Ideal Conditions:

  • Must be performed infinitesimally slowly (quasi-statically).
  • Must be free from dissipative effects (friction, viscosity, electrical resistance).

Irreversible Processes

Any real-world process that cannot be exactly reversed. All natural processes are irreversible.

Examples: Heat flow due to finite $\Delta T$, free expansion of a gas, mixing of liquids, and all processes involving friction.

Comparison Table

Feature Reversible Process Irreversible Process
Speed Infinitesimally slow (quasi-static) Proceeds at a finite rate
Work Output Maximum work output Work output is less than maximum
Entropy Change $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0$ $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$

Quick Check Questions (2)

  1. A gas expands rapidly into a vacuum. Is this process reversible or irreversible?
    See Answer
    Answer: Irreversible. The process (free expansion) occurs spontaneously and is non-quasi-static.
  2. If a process is truly reversible, what must be the change in entropy of the *universe*?
    See Answer
    Answer: $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0$. Both the system and the surroundings must return to their exact initial states.
  3. Why does the presence of friction make any real-world machine irreversible?
    See Answer
    Answer: Friction converts mechanical work into random, dissipated heat. This random heat cannot be perfectly recovered into useful work, causing an irreversible increase in the entropy of the surroundings.

3. Carnot‚Äôs Engine and Carnot‚Äôs Cycle ‚öôÔ∏è

Carnot Engine

A hypothetical, ideal, reversible heat engine operating between a Source ($T_1$) and a Sink ($T_2$). It defines the theoretical limit of efficiency.

The Carnot Cycle (Four Reversible Steps)

  1. Isothermal Expansion (A $\to$ B): Gas absorbs heat $Q_1$ from Source ($T_1$). $W_{\text{done}} = Q_1$.
  2. Adiabatic Expansion (B $\to$ C): Gas expands, $T$ drops from $T_1$ to $T_2$. $Q=0$.
  3. Isothermal Compression (C $\to$ D): Gas rejects heat $Q_2$ to Sink ($T_2$). $|W_{\text{on}}| = Q_2$.
  4. Adiabatic Compression (D $\to$ A): Gas is compressed, $T$ rises from $T_2$ back to $T_1$. $Q=0$.

Graphical Representation

The Carnot cycle on a P-V diagram (closed loop). The area enclosed is the Net Work Done ($W$).

The Carnot cycle on a T-S diagram is a perfect rectangle. Area enclosed is Net Work Done ($W$).

Derivation of Carnot Efficiency ($\eta$)

Efficiency is Work Output divided by Heat Input:

$$\eta = \frac{W}{Q_1} = \frac{Q_1 - Q_2}{Q_1} = 1 - \frac{Q_2}{Q_1}$$

For a reversible cycle, the ratio of heat exchanged equals the ratio of absolute temperatures:

$$\frac{Q_2}{Q_1} = \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$

Therefore, the maximum theoretical efficiency is:

$$\mathbf{\eta = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}}$$
Example Problem:

An engine operates between $T_1 = 500 \text{ K}$ and $T_2 = 300 \text{ K}$.

$$\eta = 1 - \frac{300}{500} = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 \quad \text{or } 40\%$$

Quick Check Questions (3)

  1. What are the four types of processes involved in a Carnot cycle?
    See Answer
    Answer: Two Isothermal processes and two Adiabatic processes.
  2. A heat engine operates between $1000 \text{ K}$ and $500 \text{ K}$. What is its maximum possible efficiency?
    See Answer
    Calculation: $\eta = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1} = 1 - \frac{500 \text{ K}}{1000 \text{ K}} = 0.5$.

    Answer: $50\%$.

  3. To increase the efficiency of a Carnot engine, is it better to increase the source temperature ($T_1$) or decrease the sink temperature ($T_2$) by the same amount ($\Delta T$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: It is always more effective to decrease the sink temperature ($T_2$). This increases the ratio $T_2/T_1$ more significantly, thus increasing $\eta = 1 - T_2/T_1$.

4. Second Law of Thermodynamics ‚öñÔ∏è

Kelvin‚ÄìPlanck Statement

It is impossible to construct an engine that, operating in a cycle, produces no effect other than the absorption of heat from a single reservoir and the performance of an equivalent amount of work.

Interpretation: $100\%$ conversion of heat to work is impossible in a cycle.

Clausius Statement

It is impossible to construct a device which operates in a cycle and produces no effect other than the transfer of heat from a colder body to a hotter body.

Interpretation: Heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot.

Equivalence of the Statements

If the Kelvin‚ÄìPlanck statement were false (a $100\%$ efficient engine existed), it could be used to violate the Clausius statement (spontaneous heat flow cold to hot), and vice versa. They are therefore logically equivalent.

Quick Check Questions (4)

  1. Which of the two statements (Kelvin‚ÄìPlanck or Clausius) directly relates to the impossibility of a Perpetual Motion Machine of the Second Kind (PMM2)?
    See Answer
    Answer: The Kelvin‚ÄìPlanck statement. A PMM2 would be an engine that achieves $100\%$ efficiency by operating from a single reservoir, which the statement forbids.
  2. If a refrigerator (a device based on the Clausius statement) is run, why is it necessary to supply external work?
    See Answer
    Answer: The Clausius statement says that heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot. To force this transfer against nature's flow, external work must be supplied.
  3. Explain the core concept that both statements aim to establish regarding natural processes.
    See Answer
    Answer: Both statements establish that all macroscopic processes are directional and cannot be perfectly reversed without leaving a change in the environment, confirming the existence of irreversibility in nature.

5. Carnot‚Äôs Theorem ‚≠ê

Statement of Carnot‚Äôs Theorem

  1. All reversible heat engines operating between the same two temperatures ($T_1$ and $T_2$) have the same efficiency.
  2. No irreversible heat engine operating between the same two temperatures can have an efficiency greater than that of a reversible engine ($\eta_{\text{irr}} < \eta_{\text{rev}}$).

Implications

The efficiency of an ideal engine depends only on the absolute temperatures ($T_1, T_2$), and not on the nature of the working substance.

Quick Check Questions (5)

  1. Engine A is a reversible engine and Engine B is an irreversible engine, both operating between $400 \text{ K}$ and $300 \text{ K}$. Can Engine B have $30\%$ efficiency if Engine A has $25\%$ efficiency?
    See Answer
    Answer: No. Carnot's Theorem states that no engine (reversible or irreversible) can be *more* efficient than the Carnot engine. The maximum possible efficiency here is $\eta_{\text{rev}} = 1 - 300/400 = 0.25$ or $25\%$. Engine A's efficiency is $25\%$, but Engine B cannot exceed $25\%$.
  2. What property of the Carnot engine makes its efficiency a universal benchmark?
    See Answer
    Answer: Its efficiency depends only on the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs ($T_1$ and $T_2$), not on the specific gas or fluid used (the working substance).

6. Thermodynamic Scale of Temperature üå°Ô∏è

Definition and Need

The Thermodynamic Scale (or Kelvin Scale) is a temperature scale based purely on the Second Law and Carnot's Theorem, making it independent of the properties of any specific substance (unlike the ideal gas scale).

Derivation

Based on Carnot's finding that the heat ratio is fixed for any reversible engine:

$$\mathbf{\frac{Q_1}{Q_2} = \frac{T_1}{T_2}}$$

This relation defines the ratio of temperatures on the thermodynamic scale.

Identity: The Thermodynamic Scale is proven to be identical to the Perfect Gas Kelvin Scale.

Quick Check Questions (6)

  1. What is the fundamental difference between the thermodynamic scale and a scale based on the expansion of alcohol or mercury?
    See Answer
    Answer: The thermodynamic scale is independent of the properties of any material, whereas scales based on alcohol or mercury depend on the specific thermal expansion properties of those substances.
  2. If a reversible engine rejects $Q_2 = 100 \text{ J}$ at $300 \text{ K}$ and absorbs $Q_1 = 150 \text{ J}$ at $T_1$, what is the temperature $T_1$ on the thermodynamic scale?
    See Answer
    Calculation: $\frac{Q_1}{Q_2} = \frac{T_1}{T_2} \implies \frac{150}{100} = \frac{T_1}{300 \text{ K}} \implies T_1 = 1.5 \times 300 \text{ K} = 450 \text{ K}$.

    Answer: $450 \text{ K}$.

7. Entropy (S) üî¨

Mathematical Definition (Change in Entropy)

The change in entropy ($dS$) for a reversible process is:

$$\mathbf{dS = \frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T}}$$

Entropy ($S$) is a state function; its value depends only on the system's state.

Physical Interpretation

  • Disorder: Entropy is a measure of the randomness, disorder, or multiplicity of microscopic states (Boltzmann: $S = k \ln W$).
  • Unavailability of Energy: It measures the energy that is unavailable for useful work.

The Principle of Increase of Entropy

The entropy of the universe always increases for any spontaneous (irreversible) process:

$$\mathbf{\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \ge 0}$$
  • Reversible: $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = 0$
  • Irreversible (Natural): $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$
Units and Dimensional Analysis:

Unit of Entropy: Joule per Kelvin ($\text{J/K}$).

$$[S] = \frac{[\text{Heat}]}{[\text{Temperature}]} = \frac{\text{M L}^2 \text{T}^{-2}}{\Theta} = \text{M L}^2 \text{T}^{-2} \Theta^{-1}$$

Quick Check Questions (7)

  1. Is entropy a state function or a path function?
    See Answer
    Answer: A state function. The change in entropy ($\Delta S$) between two states is independent of the path taken.
  2. If an ice cube melts in a glass of water (an irreversible process), what happens to the total entropy of the universe?
    See Answer
    Answer: The total entropy of the universe increases ($\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$). This confirms the process is spontaneous and irreversible.
  3. What does the Boltzmann relation, $S = k \ln W$, relate entropy to?
    See Answer
    Answer: It relates entropy to $W$, the number of microscopic configurations (disorder/multiplicity) corresponding to a macroscopic state.

8. Mathematical Formulation of the Second Law

Clausius Inequality

The mathematical form of the Second Law for any cyclic process (reversible or irreversible) is the Clausius Inequality:

$$\mathbf{\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \le 0}$$
  • If $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} = 0$, the cycle is reversible.
  • If $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} < 0$, the cycle is irreversible (and possible).

Clausius Theorem

The condition $\oint \frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T} = 0$ (for a reversible cycle) proves that $\frac{\delta Q_{\text{rev}}}{T}$ is an exact differential, defining Entropy ($S$) as a state function.

Checking Feasibility (Clausius):

A cycle takes $Q_1 = 1200 \text{ J}$ at $T_1 = 400 \text{ K}$ and rejects $Q_2 = 800 \text{ J}$ at $T_2 = 300 \text{ K}$.

$$\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} = \frac{1200}{400} + \frac{-800}{300} = 3.0 \text{ J/K} - 2.67 \text{ J/K} = 0.33 \text{ J/K}$$

Since $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} > 0$, this process is impossible (violates the Second Law).

Quick Check Questions (8)

  1. What is the sign of $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T}$ for a cycle that is theoretically possible but operates with friction?
    See Answer
    Answer: Negative ($\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} < 0$). This is the condition for an irreversible but possible cycle according to the Clausius Inequality.
  2. If a cyclic process results in $\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} = 0$, what can you conclude about the process?
    See Answer
    Answer: The process is reversible (ideal Carnot cycle or equivalent).

9. Entropy of an Ideal Gas

Entropy Change in terms of T and V

For $n$ moles of an ideal gas between states $(T_1, V_1)$ and $(T_2, V_2)$:

$$\mathbf{\Delta S = n C_v \ln\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right) + n R \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)}$$

Entropy Change in terms of T and P

For $n$ moles of an ideal gas between states $(T_1, P_1)$ and $(T_2, P_2)$ (using $C_p - C_v = R$):

$$\mathbf{\Delta S = n C_p \ln\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right) - n R \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)}$$

Quick Check Questions (9)

  1. If an ideal gas undergoes an isobaric (constant pressure) heating, which terms remain in the T-P entropy equation?
    See Answer
    Answer: The $P$ terms drop out ($\ln(P_2/P_1) = \ln(1) = 0$), leaving: $$\Delta S = n C_p \ln\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)$$
  2. Why is it acceptable to use the reversible heat capacity definitions ($C_v$ and $C_p$) to calculate the entropy change for an irreversible process between two states?
    See Answer
    Answer: Because Entropy ($S$) is a state function. The change in entropy ($\Delta S$) depends only on the initial and final states. We can therefore calculate $\Delta S$ by finding a convenient *reversible path* between the states, which simplifies the calculations using $C_v$ or $C_p$.

10. T‚ÄìS Diagram and Applications üìä

Area Under T‚ÄìS Curve

The T‚ÄìS diagram plots Absolute Temperature ($T$) against Entropy ($S$). For any reversible process, the heat absorbed ($Q_{\text{rev}}$) is given by the area under the curve:

$$Q_{\text{rev}} = \int T dS$$

Analysis of Cycles

The area enclosed by a closed cycle on the T‚ÄìS diagram represents the Net Work Done ($W$) by the cycle.

For a Carnot cycle (a rectangle): $\mathbf{W = (T_1 - T_2) \cdot \Delta S}$.

Quick Check Questions (10)

  1. What shape does a reversible cycle, such as the Carnot cycle, take on a T-S diagram?
    See Answer
    Answer: A rectangle. The isothermal paths are horizontal lines ($T$ = constant) and the adiabatic paths are vertical lines ($S$ = constant).
  2. What type of process is represented by a vertical line on a T-S diagram?
    See Answer
    Answer: A reversible Adiabatic process ($\Delta S=0$, thus Isentropic).
  3. If the area enclosed by a T-S cycle is $500 \text{ J}$, what does this value represent?
    See Answer
    Answer: The Net Work Done ($W$) by the engine during one cycle.

11. Evaluation of Entropy Changes in Simple Cases

Reversible Isothermal Expansion

Since $\Delta U = 0$, $Q_{\text{rev}} = W_{\text{rev}} = n R T \ln(V_2/V_1)$.

$$\mathbf{\Delta S = n R \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)}$$

Reversible Adiabatic Process

By definition, $Q_{\text{rev}} = 0$. Therefore, $\mathbf{\Delta S = 0}$ (Isentropic process).

Entropy Change for Phase Change

For a phase transition (e.g., melting, boiling) at constant temperature ($T_{\text{trans}}$) and pressure:

$$\mathbf{\Delta S_{\text{trans}} = \frac{L}{T_{\text{trans}}}}$$

Where $L$ is the molar or specific latent heat.

Example: Melting Ice

1 $\text{kg}$ of ice melts at $0^\circ \text{C} (273.15 \text{ K})$. Latent Heat of Fusion $L_f = 334 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

$$\Delta S = \frac{334,000 \text{ J/kg} \times 1 \text{ kg}}{273.15 \text{ K}} \approx \mathbf{1222.8 \text{ J/K}}$$

Quick Check Questions (11)

  1. If a gas doubles its volume in a reversible isothermal expansion, does the entropy increase or decrease?
    See Answer
    Answer: The entropy increases. Since $V_2 > V_1$, $\ln(V_2/V_1)$ is positive, and $\Delta S > 0$. Increased volume means increased disorder.
  2. A liquid boils at $373 \text{ K}$ with a latent heat of vaporization of $2260 \text{ kJ/kg}$. Calculate the entropy change per $\text{kg}$.
    See Answer
    Calculation: $\Delta S = \frac{L}{T} = \frac{2,260,000 \text{ J/kg}}{373 \text{ K}} \approx 6059 \text{ J/K}$.

    Answer: $6059 \text{ J/(kg¬∑K)}$.

  3. What is the entropy change of the *system* in a reversible adiabatic compression?
    See Answer
    Answer: Zero ($\Delta S = 0$). Adiabatic means $\delta Q_{\text{rev}}=0$, and the reversible condition ensures no entropy is generated.

12. Third Law of Thermodynamics ‚ùÑÔ∏è

Statement of the Third Law (Nernst Heat Theorem)

"The entropy of a pure, perfect crystalline substance is zero at the absolute zero of temperature ($0 \text{ K}$)."

$$\mathbf{S \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad T \to 0 \text{ K}}$$

Unattainability Principle

A consequence of the Third Law: "It is impossible to reduce the temperature of any system to $0 \text{ K}$ in a finite number of operations."

Significance

The Third Law provides a zero reference point for entropy, allowing the calculation of the Absolute Entropy of any substance at any temperature $T > 0 \text{ K}$.

Quick Check Questions (12)

  1. What condition must a substance meet for its entropy to be zero at $0 \text{ K}$?
    See Answer
    Answer: It must be a pure and perfect crystalline substance. Imperfect crystals or mixtures retain some residual (configurational) entropy.
  2. What is the key implication of the "Unattainability Principle"?
    See Answer
    Answer: It means that Absolute Zero ($0 \text{ K}$) can never be reached in a finite series of steps.
  3. How does the Third Law relate to the absolute value of entropy?
    See Answer
    Answer: It establishes a natural zero reference point for entropy, allowing the calculation of the absolute entropy of a substance at $T > 0 \text{ K}$, unlike internal energy ($\Delta U$) which only has relative values.

Practice Numerical Problems

  1. A heat engine absorbs $500 \text{ J}$ from a reservoir at $400 \text{ K}$ and rejects $300 \text{ J}$ to a sink at $200 \text{ K}$. Calculate the actual efficiency and the maximum possible (Carnot) efficiency.
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:
    1. Actual Efficiency ($\eta_{\text{actual}}$): $$\eta_{\text{actual}} = \frac{W}{Q_1} = \frac{Q_1 - Q_2}{Q_1}$$ $$\eta_{\text{actual}} = \frac{500 \text{ J} - 300 \text{ J}}{500 \text{ J}} = \frac{200 \text{ J}}{500 \text{ J}} = 0.4$$

      $$\mathbf{\eta_{\text{actual}} = 40\%}$$

    2. Carnot Efficiency ($\eta_{\text{Carnot}}$): $$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1}$$ $$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{200 \text{ K}}{400 \text{ K}} = 1 - 0.5 = 0.5$$

      $$\mathbf{\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 50\%}$$

    Conclusion: Since $\eta_{\text{actual}} < \eta_{\text{Carnot}}$, the engine is possible, but irreversible.

  2. $5 \text{ moles}$ of an ideal gas expand isothermally and reversibly from $1 \text{ L}$ to $10 \text{ L}$ at $300 \text{ K}$. Calculate the entropy change of the system. ($\text{R} = 8.314 \text{ J K}^{-1} \text{ mol}^{-1}$).
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:

    For an isothermal reversible expansion of an ideal gas, the entropy change is given by:

    $$\Delta S = n R \ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$ $$\Delta S = (5 \text{ mol}) \times (8.314 \text{ J K}^{-1} \text{ mol}^{-1}) \times \ln\left(\frac{10 \text{ L}}{1 \text{ L}}\right)$$ $$\Delta S \approx 41.57 \text{ J K}^{-1} \times 2.3026$$

    $$\mathbf{\Delta S \approx 95.71 \text{ J/K}}$$

    The positive sign indicates increased entropy/disorder.

  3. $1 \text{ kg}$ of water is heated from $293 \text{ K}$ to $363 \text{ K}$. Calculate the change in entropy. (Specific heat of water, $C_p = 4.184 \text{ kJ kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$). Hint: $\Delta S = m C_p \ln(T_2/T_1)$.
    See Answer
    Step-by-step Solution:

    The entropy change for a substance with constant specific heat capacity $C_p$ changing temperature is:

    $$\Delta S = m C_p \ln\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)$$

    Convert $C_p$ to $\text{J}$: $4.184 \text{ kJ kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1} = 4184 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}$.

    $$\Delta S = (1 \text{ kg}) \times (4184 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}) \times \ln\left(\frac{363 \text{ K}}{293 \text{ K}}\right)$$ $$\Delta S \approx 4184 \text{ J/K} \times \ln(1.2389)$$ $$\Delta S \approx 4184 \text{ J/K} \times 0.2144$$

    $$\mathbf{\Delta S \approx 897.0 \text{ J/K}}$$
