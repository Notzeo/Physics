Unit 3: Thermodynamic Relations

Physics Essentials

1. Thermodynamic Potentials üí°

Thermodynamic potentials are energy-like state functions that provide a concise summary of all the thermodynamic information about a system. They act as criteria for spontaneous change and equilibrium under different sets of boundary conditions.

1.1 Definitions and Physical Significance

The four potentials are defined using the fundamental equation (Combined First and Second Laws for a reversible process):

$$dU = TdS - PdV$$
Potential Definition (via State Functions) Natural Variables Meaning of Spontaneity
Internal Energy ($\mathbf{U}$) $U$ $S, V$ $dU \le 0$ at constant $S, V$
Enthalpy ($\mathbf{H}$) $H = U + PV$ $S, P$ $dH \le 0$ at constant $S, P$
Helmholtz Free Energy ($\mathbf{A}$) $A = U - TS$ $T, V$ $dA \le 0$ at constant $T, V$ (Max Work)
Gibbs Free Energy ($\mathbf{G}$) $G = H - TS$ $T, P$ $dG \le 0$ at constant $T, P$ (Max Non-PV Work)

1.2 Differential Forms and Derivations

The differential forms establish the fundamental relationships for each potential, derived from $dU = TdS - PdV$.

  • Internal Energy ($\mathbf{U}$): $$\mathbf{dU = TdS - PdV}$$
  • Enthalpy ($\mathbf{H}$): (Derived from $dH = dU + PdV + VdP$) $$\mathbf{dH = TdS + VdP}$$
  • Helmholtz Free Energy ($\mathbf{A}$): (Derived from $dA = dU - TdS - SdT$) $$\mathbf{dA = -PdV - SdT}$$
  • Gibbs Free Energy ($\mathbf{G}$): (Derived from $dG = dH - TdS - SdT$) $$\mathbf{dG = VdP - SdT}$$

1.3 Natural Variables and Thermodynamic Meaning

By comparing the differential forms to $dZ = M dX + N dY$, we obtain coefficients (e.g., $(\partial G/\partial P)_T = V$).

Key Coefficients:

From $dG = VdP - SdT$: $\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial P}\right)_T = V$ and $\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial T}\right)_P = -S$

Quick Check Questions (1)

  1. Which thermodynamic potential is the criterion for spontaneity in an isothermal and isochoric process?
    See Answer
    Answer: Helmholtz Free Energy ($\mathbf{A}$). At constant $T$ and $V$, $dA \le 0$ for spontaneity.
  2. What are the natural variables for the Enthalpy ($\mathbf{H}$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: Entropy ($\mathbf{S}$) and Pressure ($\mathbf{P}$). (Since $dH = TdS + VdP$).
  3. The change in Gibbs Free Energy ($\Delta G$) represents the maximum amount of what type of work?
    See Answer
    Answer: The maximum useful non-PV work (or electrical work, chemical work, etc.).

2. Maxwell‚Äôs Equations from Potentials üîÑ

Maxwell's relations are derived by applying the exactness condition ($\frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial X \partial Y} = \frac{\partial^2 Z}{\partial Y \partial X}$) to the four differential potentials.

2.2 Derivation of the Four Maxwell Relations

Applying the condition $\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial Y}\right)_X = \left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial X}\right)_Y$ to the differential forms:

  1. From $dU = TdS - PdV$ (Variables $S, V$): $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V}$$
  2. From $dH = TdS + VdP$ (Variables $S, P$): $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\right)_P}$$
  3. From $dA = -SdT - PdV$ (Variables $T, V$): $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V}$$
  4. From $dG = VdP - SdT$ (Variables $P, T$): $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T}$$

2.3 Physical Interpretation

Maxwell's relations are crucial as they allow us to calculate changes in hard-to-measure properties like Entropy ($\mathbf{S}$) from easily measurable laboratory data ($\mathbf{P, V, T}$).

Quick Check Questions (2)

  1. Which Maxwell relation allows you to calculate the change in entropy with respect to volume at constant temperature?
    See Answer
    Answer: The 3rd Maxwell Relation: $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$.
  2. What mathematical property of the thermodynamic potentials (state functions) makes the derivation of Maxwell's relations possible?
    See Answer
    Answer: They are exact differentials (i.e., their mixed second-order partial derivatives are equal, by Clairaut's theorem).
  3. Rewrite the 4th Maxwell relation so that the entropy term is on the right-hand side.
    See Answer
    Answer: $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$. (This is the form derived from $dG$).

3. Useful Manipulations with Partial Derivatives üìê

3.1 Cyclic and Reciprocal Relations

These identities are necessary for transforming thermodynamic derivatives into measurable coefficients.

  • Reciprocal Relation: $$\left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\right)_z = \frac{1}{\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_z}$$
  • Cyclic Relation (Triple Product Rule): $$\left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\right)_z \left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\right)_x \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)_y = -1$$

3.2 Applications: Joule-Thomson Coefficient

Using the cyclic rule and the 4th Maxwell relation, the Joule-Thomson coefficient ($\mu_{JT}$) is related to measurable quantities:

$$\mathbf{\mu_{JT} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H = \frac{1}{C_P} \left[ T\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P - V \right]}$$

3.3 Application: Adiabatic Stretching of a Wire

The combined law for a wire is $dU = TdS + FdL$ ($F$ is tension, $L$ is length). The resulting Maxwell relation is used to find temperature change during adiabatic stretching:

$$\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial L}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial S}\right)_L$$

Quick Check Questions (3)

  1. If you know the coefficient of thermal expansion $\alpha = \frac{1}{V}\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$, how can you relate this to $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_P$?
    See Answer
    Answer: Using the Reciprocal Relation: $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_P = \frac{1}{\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P} = \frac{1}{\alpha V}$.
  2. If $\mu_{JT}$ is positive for a gas, will the gas heat up or cool down upon expansion?
    See Answer
    Answer: The gas will cool down. Positive $\mu_{JT} = (\partial T/\partial P)_H$ means that a drop in pressure ($\Delta P < 0$) results in a drop in temperature ($\Delta T < 0$).

4. The Clausius‚ÄìClapeyron Equation üíßüßä

The Clausius‚ÄìClapeyron equation describes the slope of the phase boundary line on a P‚ÄìT diagram.

4.1 Derivation from Gibbs Free Energy

Starting from the equilibrium condition $\bar{G}_1 = \bar{G}_2$ and $d\bar{G} = \bar{V}dP - \bar{S}dT$, the equation is derived:

$$\mathbf{\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{\Delta \bar{H}}{T \Delta \bar{V}}}$$

Where $\Delta \bar{H}$ is the molar latent heat of the phase transition and $\Delta \bar{V}$ is the molar volume change.

4.3 P‚ÄìT Diagram and Phase Boundaries

  • Vaporization/Sublimation: $\Delta \bar{H} > 0$ and $\Delta \bar{V} > 0 \implies \mathbf{dP/dT > 0}$ (Positive slope).
  • Fusion (Water Anomalous): $\Delta \bar{H} > 0$ but $\Delta \bar{V} < 0$ (Liquid water is denser than ice) $\implies \mathbf{dP/dT < 0}$ (Negative slope).

4.4 Integrated Form (Approximate)

Assuming $\Delta \bar{H}_{\text{vap}}$ is constant and $\Delta \bar{V} \approx \bar{V}_{\text{gas}} = RT/P$, the integrated form for the Liquid $\rightleftharpoons$ Vapor transition is:

$$\mathbf{\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = - \frac{\Delta \bar{H}_{\text{vap}}}{R} \left( \frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1} \right)}$$

Quick Check Questions (4)

  1. Why does increasing pressure lower the melting point of ice, whereas increasing pressure raises the boiling point of water?
    See Answer
    Answer: The fusion curve of water has a negative slope ($dP/dT < 0$) because water is one of the few substances where the liquid phase is denser than the solid phase ($\Delta V < 0$). The boiling curve always has a positive slope.
  2. What key thermodynamic condition must be true for two phases of a substance to coexist in equilibrium at the same $T$ and $P$?
    See Answer
    Answer: Their molar Gibbs Free Energies must be equal: $\bar{G}_1 = \bar{G}_2$.

5. Triple Point üéØ

5.1 Definition and Position

The Triple Point is the unique $T$ and $P$ at which the three phases (solid, liquid, and gas) coexist in stable thermodynamic equilibrium.

5.2 Uniqueness and Significance

  • Gibbs Phase Rule: $F = C - P + 2 \implies F = 1 - 3 + 2 = 0$. This zero degree of freedom means the state is invariant (fixed $T$ and $P$).
  • Kelvin Scale: The triple point of water ($T_t = 273.16 \text{ K}$) defines the fundamental fixed point for the Kelvin scale.
Substance Triple Point Temperature (K) Triple Point Pressure (Pa)
Water $273.16$ $611.7$
Carbon Dioxide $216.55$ $518,000$

Quick Check Questions (5)

  1. Explain why the triple point of a pure substance is called an invariant point.
    See Answer
    Answer: By the Gibbs Phase Rule, the number of degrees of freedom is $F=0$. This means neither the temperature nor the pressure can be independently varied without one of the three phases disappearing.

6. Applications of Maxwell‚Äôs Thermodynamic Relations üõ†Ô∏è

Maxwell's relations are the bridge between theoretical concepts and practical measurements.

6.1 Variation of Entropy with $P$ and $V$

  • Entropy with Volume (Constant $\text{T}$): Using the 3rd Relation, for ideal gas $\Delta S = R \ln(V_2/V_1)$.
  • Entropy with Pressure (Constant $\text{T}$): Using the 4th Relation, for ideal gas $\Delta S = -R \ln(P_2/P_1)$.

6.2 Calculating Heat Capacities ($C_P$ and $C_V$)

The general relation for the difference between heat capacities, applicable to any substance:

$$\mathbf{C_P - C_V = T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P}$$

In terms of measurable coefficients (Thermal Expansivity $\alpha$ and Isothermal Compressibility $\beta_T$):

$$\mathbf{C_P - C_V = \frac{T V \alpha^2}{\beta_T}}$$

Substituting ideal gas relations into the general formula proves $\mathbf{C_P - C_V = R}$ (Mayer's Relation).

Quick Check Questions (6)

  1. The general expression for $C_P - C_V$ shows that $C_P$ must always be greater than $C_V$ (except possibly at $T=0$). What is the reasoning for this based on the formula?
    See Answer
    Answer: The term $\mathbf{\alpha^2}$ is always positive (or zero), $T$ and $V$ are positive, and the isothermal compressibility $\mathbf{\beta_T}$ must also be positive (since a system's volume must decrease when pressure increases). Thus, $\mathbf{C_P - C_V \ge 0}$.

7. Solved Numerical Examples üíª

Example 1: Using the Clausius‚ÄìClapeyron Equation

Problem: Water boils at $100 \text{^\circ}\text{C}$ under $1 \text{ atm}$ pressure. Calculate the boiling point of water at a mountain where the pressure is $0.5 \text{ atm}$. Given: $\Delta H_{\text{vap}} = 40.7 \text{ kJ/mol}$ and $R = 8.314 \text{ J mol}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}$.

Use: $\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = - \frac{\Delta \bar{H}_{\text{vap}}}{R} \left( \frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1} \right)$

Substituting values ($T_1 = 373.15 \text{ K}$):

$$\ln\left(0.5\right) = - \frac{40700}{8.314} \left( \frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{373.15} \right)$$

Solving for $T_2$ yields $T_2 \approx 354.4 \text{ K}$.

Final Answer: $T_2 \approx \mathbf{81.25 \text{^\circ}\text{C}}$.

Example 2: Checking the 3rd Maxwell Relation for an Ideal Gas

Problem: Verify the 3rd Maxwell relation, $(\partial S/\partial V)_T = (\partial P/\partial T)_V$, for an an ideal gas.

Solution:

LHS: $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \frac{1}{T} \left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T + \frac{P}{T}$

For ideal gas, $\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_T = 0$. So, $\mathbf{LHS = \frac{P}{T}}$.

RHS: From $P = RT/V$, $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{R}{V}$.

From the ideal gas law, $R/V = P/T$. So, $\mathbf{RHS = \frac{P}{T}}$.

Conclusion: LHS = RHS. The relation is verified.
