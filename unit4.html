Physics Essentials for Sunshine

1. Transport of Heat üå°Ô∏èüí®‚ú®

Introduction: Heat is a form of energy that flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature. There are three distinct modes of heat transfer: Conduction, Convection, and Radiation.

1.1 Conduction

Definition: Conduction is the process of heat transfer through a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) without any macroscopic movement of the medium's particles themselves. It primarily occurs in solids due to the vibration of lattice atoms and the movement of free electrons.

1.1.1 Law of Heat Conduction (Fourier's Law)

The rate of heat flow ($\text{H}$ or $\frac{dQ}{dt}$) through a material is:

$$\mathbf{H = \frac{dQ}{dt} = -k A \frac{dT}{dx}}$$

  • $\mathbf{H}$ (or $\frac{dQ}{dt}$): Rate of heat flow (Power) (Unit: Watt (W) or $\text{J/s}$).
  • $\mathbf{k}$: Thermal Conductivity of the material (Unit: $\text{W/m}\cdot\text{K}$).
  • $\mathbf{A}$: Area of cross-section.
  • $\mathbf{\frac{dT}{dx}}$: Temperature gradient.

Negative Sign: Indicates that heat flows in the direction of decreasing temperature.

Thermal Resistance ($\mathbf{R_T}$): Analogous to electrical resistance, for a slab of thickness $L$ and area $A$:

$$\mathbf{R_T = \frac{L}{k A}}$$

1.2 Convection

Definition: Convection is the mode of heat transfer by the actual movement of the heated matter (mass) itself. It primarily occurs in fluids (liquids and gases).

  • Natural Convection: Caused by differences in density (e.g., hot air rises).
  • Forced Convection: Caused by an external agent (e.g., fan, pump).

1.3 Radiation

Definition: Radiation is the process of heat transfer that does not require any medium. Heat is transferred in the form of electromagnetic waves (mostly infrared). It is the fastest mode of heat transfer (at the speed of light).

2. Black-Body Radiation and Thermodynamics of Radiations ‚òÄÔ∏è

2.1 Black-Body

Definition: A perfect black-body is an ideal object that absorbs all the electromagnetic radiation that falls on it. It is also a perfect emitter of radiation when heated.

2.2 Definitions in Radiations

  • Absorptive Power ($\mathbf{a}$): Ratio of energy absorbed to total energy incident. (For a perfect black-body, $a = 1$).
  • Emissive Power ($\mathbf{e}$): Radiant energy emitted per unit time per unit area.
  • Emissivity ($\mathbf{\epsilon}$): Ratio of the emissive power of a surface to that of a perfect black-body ($\text{E}_{\text{BB}}$) at the same $T$. $$\mathbf{\epsilon = \frac{e_{\text{surface}}}{E_{\text{BB}}}}$$ For a perfect black-body, $\epsilon = 1$.

2.3 Kirchhoff‚Äôs Law of Radiation

Statement: The ratio of the emissive power ($\text{e}$) to the absorptive power ($\text{a}$) is the same for all bodies and is equal to the emissive power of a perfect black-body ($\text{E}_{\text{BB}}$) at that temperature.

$$\mathbf{\frac{e}{a} = E_{\text{BB}}}$$

Conclusion: Good absorbers are good emitters.

2.4 Stefan‚ÄìBoltzmann Law

Statement: The total radiant energy ($\text{E}$) emitted per unit time per unit area of a perfect black-body is directly proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature ($\text{T}$).

$$\mathbf{E = \sigma T^4}$$

  • $\mathbf{\sigma}$: Stefan‚ÄìBoltzmann Constant ($\approx 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W/m}^2\cdot\text{K}^4$).

Net Radiative Loss: For a body at $T$ in surroundings $T_0$ (with emissivity $\epsilon$):

$$\mathbf{E_{\text{net}} = \epsilon \sigma (T^4 - T_0^4)}$$

2.5 Wien‚Äôs Displacement Law

Statement: The wavelength ($\lambda_{\text{max}}$) corresponding to the maximum energy emission is inversely proportional to the absolute temperature ($\text{T}$).

$$\mathbf{\lambda_{\text{max}} T = b}$$

Interpretation: As an object gets hotter, its color shifts from red to white/blue (shorter wavelengths).

2.6 Black-Body Spectrum ‚Äì Classical vs. Quantum

2.6.1 Rayleigh‚ÄìJeans Law (Classical Attempt)

Based on the Law of Equipartition of Energy ($\frac{1}{2} kT$ per degree of freedom, or $kT$ per mode of oscillation).

$$\mathbf{E_{\lambda} = \frac{8\pi k T}{\lambda^4}}$$

Limitation (The Ultraviolet Catastrophe): The formula incorrectly predicts that $E_{\lambda} \to \infty$ as $\lambda \to 0$ (short wavelengths), contradicting experimental data.

2.7 Quantum Theory of Radiation: Planck‚Äôs Formula

Planck proposed that energy is emitted/absorbed in discrete packets ($\text{quanta}$ or $\text{photons}$):

$$\mathbf{E = h\nu}$$

Using this quantum hypothesis, Planck derived the universal formula:

$$\mathbf{E_{\lambda} = \frac{8\pi h c}{\lambda^5} \frac{1}{e^{h c / \lambda k T} - 1}}$$

This formula accurately describes the black-body spectrum across all wavelengths.

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases (KTG) ‚öõÔ∏è

3.1 Postulates of Kinetic Theory of Gases (Key Points)

  • Gas consists of a very large number of identical, perfectly elastic, rigid molecules.
  • Molecules are in continuous, random, rapid motion.
  • The actual volume of molecules is negligible compared to the container volume.
  • No intermolecular forces except during instantaneous, elastic collisions.
  • Pressure is due to molecular collisions with the walls.
  • The average kinetic energy is directly proportional to the absolute temperature ($\text{T}$).

3.2 Kinetic Theory Interpretation of Pressure

Based on molecular collisions with container walls, the pressure ($\text{P}$) is derived as:

$$\mathbf{P V = \frac{1}{3} N m v_{\text{rms}}^2}$$

In terms of gas density ($\rho = \frac{N m}{V}$):

$$\mathbf{P = \frac{1}{3} \rho v_{\text{rms}}^2}$$

3.3 Kinetic Interpretation of Temperature

Comparing the KTG equation to the Ideal Gas Law ($PV = nRT$), the average translational kinetic energy ($\langle KE \rangle$) per molecule is found to be:

$$\mathbf{\langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k T}$$

Conclusion: Temperature is a direct measure of the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules.

3.4 Degrees of Freedom ($\mathbf{f}$) and Specific Heats

The degrees of freedom ($\text{f}$) are the total number of independent ways a molecule can possess energy (Translational, Rotational, Vibrational).

3.5 Law of Equipartition of Energy

The average energy associated with each degree of freedom is $\mathbf{\frac{1}{2} k T}$.

Molar Specific Heat at Constant Volume ($\mathbf{C_V}$):

$$\mathbf{C_V = \frac{f}{2} R}$$

Ratio of Specific Heats ($\mathbf{\gamma}$):

$$\mathbf{\gamma = \frac{C_P}{C_V} = 1 + \frac{2}{f}}$$

Gas Type $f$ $C_V$ $\gamma$
Monoatomic 3 $\frac{3}{2} R$ $\frac{5}{3} \approx 1.67$
Diatomic (Low T) 5 $\frac{5}{2} R$ $\frac{7}{5} = 1.40$

3.7 Characteristic Speeds of Molecules

The molecular speeds follow the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution $f(v)$:

$$\mathbf{f(v) dv = 4\pi \left( \frac{m}{2\pi k T} \right)^{3/2} v^2 e^{-m v^2 / 2 k T} dv}$$

Speed Name Formula
Most Probable Speed ($\mathbf{v_p}$) $$\mathbf{v_p = \sqrt{\frac{2 R T}{M}}}$$
Average Speed ($\mathbf{\langle v \rangle}$) $$\mathbf{\langle v \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{8 R T}{\pi M}}}$$
Root-Mean-Square Speed ($\mathbf{v_{\text{rms}}}$) $$\mathbf{v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3 R T}{M}}}$$

Order of Speeds: $\mathbf{v_p < \langle v \rangle < v_{\text{rms}}}$

üìö Solved Examples and Practice Question Bank

1. Transport of Heat Examples

Example 1.1 (Conduction - Thermal Resistance):

A glass window pane has an area of $0.5 \text{ m}^2$ and a thickness of $6 \text{ mm}$. If the thermal conductivity of glass is $0.8 \text{ W/m}\cdot\text{K}$, calculate the thermal resistance of the pane.

Solution: $L = 0.006 \text{ m}, A = 0.5 \text{ m}^2, k = 0.8 \text{ W/m}\cdot\text{K}$.

$$\mathbf{R_T} = \frac{L}{k A} = \frac{0.006}{(0.8)(0.5)} = \frac{0.006}{0.4} = \mathbf{0.015 \text{ K/W}}$$

2. Black-Body Radiation Examples

Example 2.2 (Wien‚Äôs Displacement Law):

The peak emission wavelength from a hot object is $500 \text{ nm}$. What is its surface temperature? ($b = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}$).

Solution: $\lambda_{\text{max}} = 500 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}$.

$$T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{\text{max}}} = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3}}{500 \times 10^{-9}} = \mathbf{5796 \text{ K}}$$

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases Examples

Example 3.2 (Degrees of Freedom and $\gamma$):

A certain gas has $\gamma = 1.4$. Calculate its molar specific heat at constant volume ($C_V$).

Solution: Use $\gamma = 1 + \frac{2}{f}$.

$$1.4 = 1 + \frac{2}{f} \implies f = \frac{2}{0.4} = 5$$ $$C_V = \frac{f}{2} R = \mathbf{\frac{5}{2} R}$$

Conceptual Short Questions (with Answers)

  1. Why is the negative sign necessary in Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction?
    See Answer
    Answer: The negative sign is necessary because it indicates that heat flow ($\mathbf{H}$) occurs in the direction of a decreasing temperature gradient ($\frac{dT}{dx}$), meaning heat flows from higher temperature to lower temperature.
  2. State the underlying assumption of the classical theory (Rayleigh‚ÄìJeans) that led to the Ultraviolet Catastrophe.
    See Answer
    Answer: The classical theory assumed that each mode of oscillation (degree of freedom) in the black-body cavity possessed a mean energy of $\mathbf{kT}$ (based on the Law of Equipartition of Energy), regardless of frequency. This led to infinite energy at short wavelengths.
  3. How does the average kinetic energy of a gas molecule change if the absolute temperature is quadrupled?
    See Answer
    Answer: Since $\mathbf{\langle KE \rangle = \frac{3}{2} k T}$, the average kinetic energy is directly proportional to the absolute temperature. If $T$ is quadrupled (multiplied by 4), the average kinetic energy will also be quadrupled.
  4. Explain why a black surface gets hotter than a white surface under direct sunlight (relate to Kirchhoff's Law).
    See Answer
    Answer: A black surface has a high absorptive power ($\mathbf{a \approx 1}$) for visible sunlight. According to Kirchhoff's Law ($\mathbf{\frac{e}{a} = E_{\text{BB}}}$), good absorbers are good emitters. A white surface is a poor absorber ($\mathbf{a \approx 0}$), reflecting most light. Since the black surface absorbs much more incident energy, its temperature rises higher.
  5. What are the three types of speeds in the Maxwell distribution, and which is the smallest?
    See Answer
    Answer: The three speeds are the Most Probable Speed ($\mathbf{v_p}$), the Average Speed ($\mathbf{\langle v \rangle}$), and the Root-Mean-Square Speed ($\mathbf{v_{\text{rms}}}$). The Most Probable Speed ($\mathbf{v_p}$) is the smallest.

Numerical Problems (with Answers)

  1. The emissive power of a body is $100 \text{ W/m}^2$ at a certain temperature, and its absorptive power is $0.25$. What is the emissive power of a black-body ($\text{E}_{\text{BB}}$) at the same temperature?
    See Answer
    Calculation: Using Kirchhoff‚Äôs Law: $\frac{e}{a} = E_{\text{BB}}$. $$E_{\text{BB}} = \frac{100 \text{ W/m}^2}{0.25} = 400 \text{ W/m}^2$$ Answer: The emissive power of a black-body is $\mathbf{400 \text{ W/m}^2}$.
  2. A furnace radiates maximum energy at a wavelength of $4.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}$. What is the temperature of the furnace? ($b = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}$).
    See Answer
    Calculation: Using Wien's Law: $\lambda_{\text{max}} T = b$. $$T = \frac{b}{\lambda_{\text{max}}} = \frac{2.898 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}\cdot\text{K}}{4.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}} = 0.7245 \times 10^3 = 724.5 \text{ K}$$ Answer: The temperature of the furnace is $\mathbf{724.5 \text{ K}}$.
  3. Calculate the ratio of the RMS speed of Helium molecules ($\mathbf{M=4}$ $\text{g/mol}$) to that of Oxygen molecules ($\mathbf{M=32}$ $\text{g/mol}$) at the same temperature.
    See Answer
    Calculation: $v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3 R T}{M}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}$. $$\frac{(v_{\text{rms}})_{\text{He}}}{(v_{\text{rms}})_{\text{O}_2}} = \sqrt{\frac{M_{\text{O}_2}}{M_{\text{He}}}} = \sqrt{\frac{32}{4}} = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$$ Answer: The ratio is $\mathbf{2\sqrt{2}}$ (or approximately $2.828:1$).
  4. If the molar specific heat at constant volume ($\mathbf{C_V}$) for an unknown gas is $3 R$, find its degrees of freedom $f$ and the ratio of specific heats $\gamma$.
    See Answer
    Calculation for $\mathbf{f}$ (Degrees of Freedom): $$C_V = \frac{f}{2} R \implies 3 R = \frac{f}{2} R \implies f = 6$$ Calculation for $\mathbf{\gamma}$: $$\gamma = 1 + \frac{2}{f} = 1 + \frac{2}{6} = 1 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{3}$$ Answer: $f = \mathbf{6}$ (likely a triatomic non-linear gas like $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ at moderate $\text{T}$), and $\gamma = \mathbf{\frac{4}{3}} \approx 1.33$.

Objective-type Questions (with Answers)

  1. The ratio of $\mathbf{\langle v \rangle : v_{\text{rms}}}$ is:
    See Answer
    Answer: (b) $\mathbf{\sqrt{8/3\pi}}$. $$\frac{\langle v \rangle}{v_{\text{rms}}} = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{8 R T}{\pi M}}}{\sqrt{\frac{3 R T}{M}}} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{3\pi}}$$
  2. At high temperatures, a diatomic gas has $\gamma = \dots$ ($\frac{7}{5}$ / $\frac{9}{7}$ / $\frac{5}{3}$).
    See Answer
    Answer: $\mathbf{\frac{9}{7}}$. At high T, $f=7$ (3 translational + 2 rotational + 2 vibrational). $\gamma = 1 + \frac{2}{7} = \frac{9}{7}$.
  3. The unit of Stefan‚ÄìBoltzmann constant $\mathbf{\sigma}$ is $\dots$.
    See Answer
    Answer: $\mathbf{W/m}^2\cdot\mathbf{K}^4$.
  4. The failure of classical theory to explain the black-body spectrum at short wavelengths is known as the $\dots$.
    See Answer
    Answer: Ultraviolet Catastrophe.
