1. Thermodynamic Potentials üåü

Concept of Potentials

Thermodynamic potentials are state functions that represent the **stored energy** in a system under specific constant constraints (T, V, P, or S). Their differentials (e.g., $dU, dH, dF, dG$) relate the First and Second Laws.

1.1 Internal Energy ($U$)

Represents the total energy of a system. It is the natural potential for processes at **constant $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ (isentropic/isochoric)**.

$$\mathbf{dU = T dS - P dV}$$

1.2 Enthalpy ($H$)

Defined as $H = U + PV$. It is the natural potential for processes at **constant $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{P}$ (isentropic/isobaric)**.

$$\mathbf{dH = T dS + V dP}$$
Key Use: $\Delta H$ is the heat exchanged ($\Delta Q$) in an **isobaric** (constant pressure) process.

1.3 Helmholtz Free Energy ($F$)

Defined as $F = U - TS$. It is the natural potential for processes at **constant $\mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ (isothermal/isochoric)**.

$$\mathbf{dF = -S dT - P dV}$$
Key Use: $-\Delta F$ represents the **maximum work** ($W_{\text{max}}$) a system can perform during an isothermal process.

1.4 Gibbs Free Energy ($G$)

Defined as $G = H - TS = U + PV - TS$. It is the natural potential for processes at **constant $\mathbf{T}$ and $\mathbf{P}$ (isothermal/isobaric)**.

$$\mathbf{dG = -S dT + V dP}$$
Key Use: $\Delta G$ determines the **spontaneity** of a process at constant T and P. $\Delta G < 0$ means spontaneous.

Quick Check Questions (1)

  1. Which thermodynamic potential is the most useful criterion for spontaneity in a chemical reaction that occurs in an open beaker?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Gibbs Free Energy ($\mathbf{G}$)**. An open beaker is typically at constant temperature ($T$) and constant pressure ($P$).
  2. If an engine performs work at constant volume and temperature, which potential determines the maximum work done?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Helmholtz Free Energy ($\mathbf{F}$)**. $-\Delta F$ is the maximum work at constant $T$ and $V$.

2. Maxwell‚Äôs Relations (From Potentials) üîó

Concept

The four Maxwell relations are a set of equations derived from the fact that the potentials ($U, H, F, G$) are **exact differentials** (state functions). This means the order of mixed partial differentiation does not matter.

Derivation Principle

For a state function $Z(x, y)$, where $dZ = M dx + N dy$, we have $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$.

The Four Relations

  1. **From $U$ ($dU = T dS - P dV$):** $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V}$$
  2. **From $H$ ($dH = T dS + V dP$):** $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\right)_P}$$
  3. **From $F$ ($dF = -S dT - P dV$):** $$\mathbf{\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V}$$
  4. **From $G$ ($dG = -S dT + V dP$):** $$\mathbf{-\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P}$$
Significance: Maxwell's relations allow us to relate difficult-to-measure properties (like $\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V$) to easily measurable properties (like $\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T$).

Quick Check Questions (2)

  1. Which Maxwell relation connects the entropy and pressure changes with temperature and volume?
    See Answer
    Answer: The **4th relation (from G)**: $-\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P$.
  2. What is the mathematical condition that makes the Maxwell relations possible?
    See Answer
    Answer: The thermodynamic potentials ($U, H, F, G$) must be **exact differentials**, meaning their second-order mixed partial derivatives are equal.

3. Phase Transitions and Clausius-Clapeyron Equation üíß

3.1 Phase Equilibrium

Two phases (e.g., liquid and vapor) are in equilibrium when they have the same **temperature, pressure, and Gibbs free energy per mole ($\mathbf{G_m}$)**.

$$\mathbf{G_{\text{phase 1}} = G_{\text{phase 2}}}$$

3.2 Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

This equation describes the slope of the phase boundary curve ($\frac{dP}{dT}$) on a P-T diagram.

It relates the latent heat of transition ($\mathbf{L}$), the temperature ($\mathbf{T}$), and the change in volume ($\mathbf{\Delta V}$) during the transition.

$$\mathbf{\frac{dP}{dT} = \frac{L}{T \Delta V}}$$
  • $\mathbf{L}$: Latent heat (e.g., $L_{\text{fusion}}, L_{\text{vaporization}}$).
  • $\mathbf{\Delta V = V_2 - V_1}$: Change in volume between the two phases.

3.3 Application to Vaporization

When liquid converts to vapor, $V_{\text{vapor}} \gg V_{\text{liquid}}$, so $\Delta V \approx V_{\text{vapor}}$. Also, $P V_{\text{vapor}} = R T$.

The **integrated form** (assuming $L$ is constant and applying the Ideal Gas Law) is:

$$\mathbf{\ln P = -\frac{L}{R T} + C}$$
Prediction: Since $L > 0$ and $\Delta V > 0$ (for vaporization), $\frac{dP}{dT} > 0$. **Boiling point increases with pressure.**

3.4 Application to Fusion (Melting)

  • **Most Substances ($\mathbf{V_{\text{liquid}} > V_{\text{solid}}}$):** $\Delta V > 0 \implies \frac{dP}{dT} > 0$. Melting point increases with pressure.
  • **Water ($\mathbf{V_{\text{liquid}} < V_{\text{solid}}}$):** $\Delta V < 0 \implies \frac{dP}{dT} < 0$. **Melting point decreases with pressure** (this is why ice skating works).

Quick Check Questions (3)

  1. What must be equal between two phases in equilibrium (besides $T$ and $P$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: The **Gibbs Free Energy per mole ($\mathbf{G_m}$)**.
  2. If the Clausius-Clapeyron equation gives a negative slope ($\frac{dP}{dT} < 0$), what does that tell you about the relative volumes of the two phases?
    See Answer
    Answer: It means the **volume of Phase 1 is greater than the volume of Phase 2 ($\mathbf{\Delta V < 0}$)**. For example, ice (Phase 1) melting into water (Phase 2).

4. Joule-Thomson Effect (Porous Plug Experiment) ‚ùÑÔ∏è

Concept

When a real gas is forced to flow steadily through a **porous plug** from a high-pressure region ($P_1$) to a low-pressure region ($P_2$) under **adiabatic** conditions, a temperature change is generally observed.

Key Property: This is an **Isenthalpic** process ($H = \text{constant}$), meaning the enthalpy does not change ($\Delta H = 0$).

Joule-Thomson Coefficient ($\mu$)

It is defined as the change in temperature with respect to pressure at constant enthalpy:

$$\mathbf{\mu = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H}$$
  • $\mathbf{\mu > 0}$: Cooling (Observed for most gases at room temperature).
  • $\mathbf{\mu < 0}$: Heating (Observed for $\text{H}_2$ and $\text{He}$ above their inversion temperature).
  • $\mathbf{\mu = 0}$: No change in temperature.

Inversion Temperature ($T_i$)

The temperature at which $\mu$ changes sign ($\mu = 0$).

  • Above $T_i$, the gas heats upon expansion ($\mu < 0$).
  • Below $T_i$, the gas cools upon expansion ($\mu > 0$).
Liquefaction Principle: To liquefy a gas using the Joule-Thomson effect (e.g., in a Linde machine), the gas must first be cooled **below its inversion temperature ($T_i$)**. $\text{H}_2$ and $\text{He}$ require precooling because their $T_i$ is very low.

Quick Check Questions (4)

  1. If a gas experiences a cooling effect ($\Delta T < 0$) during Joule-Thomson expansion, what is the sign of the Joule-Thomson coefficient ($\mu$)?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Positive ($\mathbf{\mu > 0}$)**. A decrease in $P$ ($\Delta P < 0$) causes a decrease in $T$ ($\Delta T < 0$), making the ratio $\mu = \Delta T / \Delta P$ positive.
  2. What is the conserved quantity in a Joule-Thomson expansion?
    See Answer
    Answer: **Enthalpy ($\mathbf{H}$)**. The process is isenthalpic ($\Delta H = 0$).
