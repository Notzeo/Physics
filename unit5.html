Unit 5: Fundamentals of Statistical Mechanics

The Bridge from Micro to Macro

1. Introduction to Statistical Mechanics üß†

1.1 Definition and Scope

Statistical Mechanics (SM): It is a branch of physics that seeks to explain the macroscopic (thermodynamic) properties of matter in terms of the behavior and properties of its microscopic constituents (atoms and molecules).

Scope: SM provides a statistical interpretation of fundamental thermodynamic quantities like temperature, pressure, and entropy, deriving them from the laws of mechanics (classical or quantum) applied to a system with an enormous number of particles ($\approx 10^{23}$).

1.2 Importance of Statistics in Thermodynamics

Large Number Problem: A macroscopic system contains an astronomically large number of particles ($N$). It's impossible to track the state ($\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{p}_i$) of every single particle.

Statistical Necessity: Since we cannot track individuals, we rely on statistical averages and probability to predict the bulk behavior of the system. For large $N$, statistical predictions become virtually certain (i.e., fluctuations are negligible).

1.3 Relationship between Microscopic and Macroscopic Systems

System Description Variables
Microscopic Deals with individual particles and their mechanical states. Position ($\mathbf{r}_i$), Momentum ($\mathbf{p}_i$), Energy ($\epsilon_i$).
Macroscopic Deals with the system as a whole (bulk properties). Pressure ($P$), Volume ($V$), Temperature ($T$), Entropy ($S$).

The Bridge: Statistical Mechanics provides the rules to transition from the probabilities of microscopic states to the average values observed as macroscopic properties.

2. Probability and Entropy: The Statistical Postulate üìú

2.1 Concept of Probability and Thermodynamic Probability

a) Probability ($P$)

Definition: In general, the probability of an event is the ratio of the number of favorable outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes.

$$P = \frac{\text{Number of Favorable Outcomes}}{\text{Total Number of Possible Outcomes}}$$

b) Thermodynamic Probability ($W$)

Definition: The Thermodynamic Probability ($W$) (or Multiplicity) of a given macrostate is the total number of distinct microstates corresponding to that macrostate.

Nature: Unlike mathematical probability ($P \le 1$), $W$ is always an integer and usually a very large number.

Mathematical Expression (Distinguishable Particles):

$$W = \frac{N!}{n_1! n_2! \cdots n_k!} = \frac{N!}{\prod_{i=1}^{k} n_i!}$$

Constraint: $\sum_{i=1}^{k} n_i = N$ (Total number of particles is constant).

Example: Distributing 3 distinguishable particles (A, B, C) into 2 boxes such that $n_1=2$ and $n_2=1$. $$W = \frac{3!}{2! 1!} = 3$$

Possible microstates: $\{(A, B) | C\}$, $\{(A, C) | B\}$, $\{(B, C) | A\}$.

2.2 Connection between Probability and Entropy

Fundamental Insight: A system naturally evolves toward the macrostate that has the highest thermodynamic probability ($\mathbf{W}$), as this state is statistically the most likely.

Equilibrium: The state of thermodynamic equilibrium is the macrostate for which $W$ is maximum ($W_{\max}$).

Boltzmann's Postulate: The macroscopic quantity Entropy ($S$) is directly related to the maximum thermodynamic probability by:

$$\mathbf{S = k \ln W}$$

$k$: Boltzmann constant ($k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J/K}$).

Since a system naturally moves toward $W_{\max}$, and $\ln W$ is an increasing function, the system naturally moves toward $\mathbf{S_{\max}}$. This provides a statistical basis for the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

3. Postulates and Ensembles üåå

3.1 Fundamental Assumptions

Postulate of Equal a Priori Probability (Microcanonical Ensemble)

Statement: For an isolated system in equilibrium, all accessible microstates corresponding to the given macrostate (defined by $E, V, N$) are equally probable.

Meaning: If a system can be in $W$ different microstates, the probability of finding the system in any one of those specific microstates is exactly $1/W$.

Ergodic Hypothesis (Conceptual)

Statement: For a system in equilibrium, the time average of any physical quantity (taken as the system evolves over a long time) is equal to the ensemble average (taken over all microstates accessible to the system at any given instant).

Significance: It justifies replacing complex time averages with simpler ensemble averages.

3.2 Concept of Ensembles (Gibbs)

Ensemble: A hypothetical collection of a very large number of independent systems, all constructed to be microscopic copies of the actual system under consideration, but differing in their instantaneous microscopic state.

Representative Point: The instantaneous microscopic state of a system is uniquely defined by its coordinates in phase space. The collection of these points represents the statistical distribution of the ensemble.

3.3 Types of Ensembles (The Three Pillars)

Ensemble System Type Fixed Variables Statistical Weight / Probability ($\mathbf{P_i}$) Associated Potential
Microcanonical Isolated $N, V, E$ $P_i = 1/\Omega(E)$ (Constant) Entropy ($S$)
Canonical Closed (Heat Reservoir) $N, V, T$ $P_i \propto e^{-E_i / k T}$ (Boltzmann Factor) Helmholtz Free Energy ($A$)
Grand Canonical Open (Heat & Particle Reservoir) $V, T, \mu$ $P_i \propto e^{-(E_i - \mu N_i) / k T}$ Grand Potential ($\Phi$)

4. Macrostates and Microstates üî¨

4.1 Definition and Difference

  • Macrostate: The state of a system specified by its macroscopic parameters ($P, V, T, E, N$). It describes the observable, bulk properties.
  • Microstate: The state of a system specified by the exact state of every single constituent particle (position and momentum, or quantum state).
Feature Macrostate Microstate
Specification Bulk variables ($P, V, T, E$) Particle variables ($\mathbf{r}_i, \mathbf{p}_i$)
Number Few Extremely Large ($W$)
Observability Directly Observable Hypothetical/Unobservable

4.2 Thermodynamic Probability ($W$) Example (4 Coins)

For $N=4$ distinguishable coins, the macrostate is defined by the count of Heads ($H$) and Tails ($T$). The most probable macrostate has the highest $W$ (or Multiplicity).

Macrostate ($n_H, n_T$) Thermodynamic Probability ($W$)
(4, 0) 1
(3, 1) 4
(2, 2) $\mathbf{6}$ ($W_{\max}$)
(1, 3) 4
(0, 4) 1

As $N \to \infty$, the distribution becomes sharply peaked around the equilibrium mean ($N/2, N/2$), confirming that equilibrium is the most probable state.

5. Phase Space and Elementary Cells üåå

5.1 Definition of Phase Space

Phase Space: A theoretical, multi-dimensional space used to represent the instantaneous state of a mechanical system by its generalized position coordinates ($q_i$) and conjugate momentum coordinates ($p_i$).

Dimensions: For a system with $N$ particles in 3D, the phase space is $\mathbf{6N}$-dimensional ($3N$ position $+ 3N$ momentum).

Representative Point: Every microstate corresponds to a single point in $\gamma$-space. The evolution of the system is the trajectory of this point.

5.2 $\mu$-space and $\gamma$-space

  • $\mu$-space (Mu-space, Single-Particle): 6-dimensional (3 position, 3 momentum). The state of the entire $N$-particle system is represented by $N$ separate points in $\mu$-space.
  • $\gamma$-space (Gamma-space, Multi-Particle): $6N$-dimensional. The state of the entire system is represented by a single, unique representative point.

5.3 Elementary Cells and Quantum Connection

Elementary Cell (Quantum Volume): Due to the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle ($\Delta q \Delta p \ge h$), the phase space cannot be specified with infinite precision. It must be divided into tiny, irreducible volumes called elementary cells.

Volume of Elementary Cell:

  • In 6-dimensional $\mu$-space: $\mathbf{\text{Volume} = h^3}$
  • In $6N$-dimensional $\gamma$-space: $\mathbf{\text{Volume} = h^{3N}}$

Counting the number of microstates ($W$) is equivalent to counting the number of elementary cells occupied by the system, linking statistical mechanics to quantum mechanics via Planck's constant ($h$).

6. Equilibrium and Fluctuation Constraints üå°Ô∏è

6.1 Concept of Equilibrium in Statistical Mechanics

Equilibrium: The most probable macrostate ($W_{\max}$) toward which the system naturally evolves. Macroscopic parameters are stable over time.

Mathematical Condition: Equilibrium is found by maximizing $\ln W$ subject to constraints: $\sum n_i = N$ (constant particles) and $\sum n_i \epsilon_i = E$ (constant energy).

6.2 Conditions for Equilibrium Between Systems

When two systems $A_1$ and $A_2$ exchange a quantity $X$ (like energy, volume, or particles), the equilibrium condition (maximizing total entropy $S = S_1 + S_2$) is achieved when the intensive parameters associated with $X$ are equal.

  • Thermal Equilibrium ($\delta E = 0$): Since $\frac{\partial S}{\partial E} = \frac{1}{T}$, equilibrium requires: $$\mathbf{T_1 = T_2}$$
  • Mechanical Equilibrium ($\delta V = 0$): Since $\frac{\partial S}{\partial V} = \frac{P}{T}$, equilibrium requires: $$\mathbf{P_1 = P_2}$$
  • Diffusive Equilibrium ($\delta N = 0$): Since $\frac{\partial S}{\partial N} = -\frac{\mu}{T}$, equilibrium requires: $$\mathbf{\mu_1 = \mu_2}$$ (Chemical Potential is equal).

6.3 Fluctuations and Statistical Basis of the Second Law

Fluctuations: Deviations from the equilibrium state ($W_{\max}$). They are negligible for macroscopic systems ($N \to \infty$).

Probability of Fluctuation: The probability of observing a state with entropy $S$ that is slightly less than the maximum equilibrium entropy $S_0$ is given by:

$$P(S) \propto e^{(S - S_0) / k}$$

Since $S_0 - S$ is positive, the probability of any large fluctuation (decrease in $S$) is exponentially tiny. The Second Law ($\Delta S \ge 0$) is thus a statement of overwhelmingly high probability, not absolute certainty.

7. Boltzmann Canonical Distribution Law üå°Ô∏è

7.1 Expression for Probability ($P_i$)

The canonical distribution gives the probability $P_i$ that a system, in thermal contact with a large heat reservoir at constant temperature $T$, will be in a specific microstate $i$ with energy $E_i$.

$$P_i = \frac{e^{-E_i / k T}}{\sum_{j} e^{-E_j / k T}} = \frac{e^{-E_i / k T}}{Z}$$

7.2 Partition Function ($Z$) and its Significance

Definition (Sum over microstates):

$$Z = \sum_{i} e^{-E_i / k T}$$

Significance (The Bridge): All macroscopic thermodynamic properties can be derived from the Partition Function ($Z$) and its derivatives:

  • Normalization: $Z$ is the normalization constant ($\sum P_i = 1$).
  • Helmholtz Free Energy ($A$): $$\mathbf{A = - k T \ln Z}$$
  • Internal Energy ($U$): $U = \langle E \rangle = - \left( \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \right)_{V} \quad \text{where } \beta = 1/kT$
Example: Population Ratio

The ratio of the number of particles ($n_2/n_1$) occupying two non-degenerate energy levels $\epsilon_2$ and $\epsilon_1$ ($\epsilon_2 > \epsilon_1$) at temperature $T$ is given by the Boltzmann factor ratio:

$$\frac{n_2}{n_1} = e^{-(\epsilon_2 - \epsilon_1) / k T}$$

8. Classical and Quantum Statistics ‚öõÔ∏è

8.1 Need for Quantum Statistics

Classical Maxwell-Boltzmann (MB) statistics failed because it treated particles as distinguishable, which violates the fundamental quantum reality of indistinguishable particles (electrons, photons, etc.), and it ignored the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

8.2 Comparison of the Three Statistics

Feature Maxwell‚ÄìBoltzmann (MB) Bose‚ÄìEinstein (BE) Fermi‚ÄìDirac (FD)
Distinguishability Distinguishable (Classical) Indistinguishable (Quantum) Indistinguishable (Quantum)
Particle Type Ideal Gases (High $T$) Bosons (Integer Spin) Fermions (Half-Integer Spin)
Occupancy Unlimited Unlimited Max 1 particle per state (Pauli)
Distribution Law ($n_i/g_i$) $\propto e^{-\epsilon_i / k T}$ $\frac{1}{e^{(\epsilon_i - \mu) / k T} - 1}$ $\frac{1}{e^{(\epsilon_i - \mu) / k T} + 1}$
Key Concepts:
  • Bose-Einstein Condensation (BEC): At very low $T$, a macroscopic number of bosons "condense" into the lowest energy state.
  • Fermi Energy ($E_F$): At $T=0 \text{ K}$, all energy levels up to $E_F$ are completely filled by fermions, and all above are empty.

Unit 5 Review Questions

  1. Explain the physical meaning of the ratio $\frac{\sqrt{\langle (\Delta E)^2 \rangle}}{\langle E \rangle} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}$ in statistical mechanics.
    See Answer
    Answer: This ratio represents the relative energy fluctuation. Since $N$ is astronomically large ($\approx 10^{23}$), the fluctuation is incredibly small, which justifies the use of time-independent equilibrium values for macroscopic systems.
  2. What property of entropy dictates that the function $S=f(W)$ must be logarithmic?
    See Answer
    Answer: Entropy must be an additive property ($S = S_1 + S_2$), while thermodynamic probability is a multiplicative property ($W = W_1 \times W_2$). The only function satisfying $f(W_1 W_2) = f(W_1) + f(W_2)$ is $f(W) = k \ln W$.
  3. What is the significance of the fact that all accessible microstates are equally probable in the Microcanonical Ensemble?
    See Answer
    Answer: This postulate (Equal a Priori Probability) ensures that when a system is isolated (fixed $N, V, E$), there is no physical bias towards any specific microscopic arrangement, allowing the calculation of ensemble averages to be straightforward and statistically unbiased.
  4. Which physical principle makes the concept of an elementary cell in phase space necessary?
    See Answer
    Answer: The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle ($\Delta q \Delta p \ge h$), which dictates that position and momentum cannot be simultaneously known with infinite precision, defining a minimum, irreducible volume $h$ for a pair of canonical coordinates.
